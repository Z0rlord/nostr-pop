# Yawara (柔)

**Unarmed grappling and restraint** — called **Yawara** (柔) on the international site and **jujutsu** (柔術) in Japanese official material. One of eight major arts in Tenshinryu hyoho (see [[arts/_index]]).

## Naming

| Term | Usage |
|------|--------|
| **Yawara** (柔) | International site, historical soft-hand reading |
| **Jujutsu** (柔術) | tenshinryu.net curriculum list |

This wiki uses slug **yawara** to match the international eight-art list; content covers the same unarmed line.

## Curriculum placement

Japanese official text classifies **jujutsu** as **tonomono / heiden** (外物・併伝) — supplementary to core **denchu toho**, batto, and kenjutsu. It is transmitted by stage and student interest, not as an isolated entry art (see [[arts/_index]]).

## Seated forms and wakizashi

A distinctive Tenshinryu rule: even in **seated** unarmed forms (*za-hō*), practitioners wear the **wakizashi**. Most jujutsu schools remove all weapons for grappling; Tenshinryu treats daily samurai dress as non-negotiable so hip equipment, entanglement, and emergency draw are trained realistically (see [[philosophy/kata-culture]] and [[arts/battojutsu]]).

## Role relative to sword arts

Unarmed methods address situations where the sword cannot be drawn — restraint, indoor proximity, surprise at zero maai, or failure of the draw. Hyoho assumes **sleeping, waking, sitting, standing, walking, and running** as combat contexts; yawara completes the matrix when blade and long weapon are unavailable or inappropriate.

## Training notes

Instructor material distinguishes correct form practice (understanding the attacker's grip intent) from sloppy paired drill — a common failure mode in jujutsu as in kenjutsu (see [[philosophy/kata-culture]]).
