# The Flow of Training

﻿The Flow of Training
Kuwami Masakumo
________________
Here we present the actual training procedures and flow. You do not need to follow this to the letter. Use it as a reference and feel free to adapt it to the circumstances at the time; also consider the available training time and establish methods suited to each dojo.
What matters is to preserve the tradition as much as possible while continually improving training quality. By 'training quality' we mean students' improvement, students' physical conditioning, students' acquisition of knowledge, and students' satisfaction with training.
Preparation for Training
First, make sure you are already fully prepared by the time training begins. Ideally, unless prevented by unavoidable circumstances such as work, you should be at the dojo before anyone else.
Who is the person most excited about training? In principle, it is the one who arrives first. People waiting for Disneyland to open, lining up at the Apple Store for a new iPhone, queueing for Comiket, or waiting for the release of Dragon Quest—those who stand at the front of the line have the highest motivation.
That said, unless one is a full-time instructor, one also has work, family, and various obligations; consequently, advance preparation tends to fall short, and it becomes all too easy to lapse into inertia.
Even so, from the standpoint of hyoho (兵法 (martial strategy), the norm is to prepare thoroughly and be ready in advance. Miyamoto Musashi’s tactic of deliberate lateness was effective only in exceptional cases; as a regular practice, it is not desirable.
Accordingly, arrive early and get yourself set if at all possible. Then review the training content and the timetable. Confirming techniques is especially important.
As Ishii Sensei (8th Shike) says, ‘Do not teach anything other than what you truly have confidence in.’ Tenshinryu has many techniques, and it is easy to be unsure of some. Check in advance as much as possible so that you do not make mistakes during instruction. We are currently formulating monthly training menus. However, there may still be items whose details are not yet known. The menu is designed with enough volume that even if you omit such items, you can still conduct a full practice. Select and prioritize as appropriate for the situation. We will also be producing technical manuals to support instruction.
* Changing into training wear
* Preparing training equipment
* Tasks related to facility use (including writing any required reports)
* Checking the timetable, lesson content, and confirming techniques
* Pre-class cleaning of the facility (as needed)
One of the few points about Tenshin Sensei that is especially worth emulating is early arrival. Tenshin Sensei generally made a point of arriving an hour beforehand.
When students arrive, take the initiative to greet them yourself. Provide guidance to trial participants and observers, or, if you are busy, ask other students to assist. Neglect is the one thing to avoid most.
Pay special attention to following up with new members so they do not feel ill at ease. Balance is also crucial. This applies across the board: if you focus too much on veterans, or too much on brand-new members, or too much on trial participants and observers, you lose balance and the overall result becomes negative.
Even if you are short-handed and your attention becomes uneven, if you see signs of that in advance, say so, and be sure to follow up afterward. For example, if you had to spend time with trial participants, observers, or new members, clearly communicate during or afterward that you were unable to attend to others and that you are sorry, and then make every effort to restore balance as promptly as possible. Remember that ‘it can’t be helped’ is something the other party may say to forgive you; it is not a phrase you should use as your own excuse.
Because you cannot spend all your time on trial participants, observers, or new members, ask them to imitate what they see when appropriate, or ask veteran members to keep an eye on them, and take care not to leave anyone simply unattended. A brief verbal correction at the right time, praising them when there is no problem, or simply maintaining clear eye contact will dispel anxiety.
Also, during casual conversation in practice, lightly introduce individual members. This elevates others from ‘someone I don’t know’ to ‘someone whose story or character I know,’ which increases a sense of reassurance and familiarity. Even when you delegate watchfulness to veteran members, do not just leave things to them—make rounds yourself in addition to the methods above. And do not forget to show consideration to the veterans who are helping by keeping watch.
We may also include wiping the floor with rags (Zōkin-gake 雑巾がけ run in a low crouch while pushing a floor cloth). Although it is uncertain whether this was done in former times (the Edo period), for modern practitioners it provides extremely effective lower-body conditioning and is also ideal for cleaning the dojo.
On Warm-Up Exercises
In principle, Tenshinryu does not include formal warm-up exercises. Basic training already contains the necessary warming elements, daily practice time used to be ample, and above all, unlike today, people had far more opportunities to use their bodies in everyday life.
In the modern era, however, people have fewer opportunities and less time for physical activity. As a result, the standard training menu alone may be insufficient, and doing warm-up exercises before practice can be very useful for preventing injuries.
Tenshin Sensei, for example, disliked group calisthenics and generally did not permit them from the standpoint of tradition. At the same time, he instructed individuals to do their own warm-ups so as not to get injured. It may sound contradictory, but the point is: the school should not conduct warm-ups officially as a matter of course; rather, each person should make appropriate preparations in advance, exercising their own judgment according to the situation.
How much time to devote is up to each dojo when allocating practice time, and it is acceptable to run a group warm-up if desired. That said, not everyone can assemble exactly at the start time, so individual warm-ups before class may well be sufficient. It is advisable to allow some buffer before the official start time and have each person do their own warm-up as part of getting their gear and training setup ready. Many instructors themselves are short on exercise; if the instructors take the lead in warming up, students will naturally follow suit.
Suggested time: about 5–10 minutes.
Start of Practice
1. Zarei 坐礼 (Gassyu-re 合手礼)
2. Ken-za-zen 剣坐禅 (meditation)
3. Kinchō 金打
At the start of practice, first form ranks and perform Zarei 坐礼 (Gassyu-re 合手礼).
Either before or after the bow, offer a brief chat or short talk to set the tone for the day’s training. This is an icebreaker, and it is trickier than it looks. If there are announcements, convey them; if not, share something witty or engaging. Saying ‘we’re just here to train, so let’s simply begin’ is an evasion that ignores human psychology. When the matter is not important, or if some students are still preparing, speak before the bow; otherwise, do it after the bow.
If time permits, follow with Ken-za-zen 剣坐禅 (meditation). There is no fixed length, but about five minutes is typical. Even a short period is fine; do it whenever possible. Ken-za-zen is a meditation that temporarily separates mind and body from the bustle of daily life and focuses them on training, thereby enhancing the effectiveness of practice. After that, conduct the Kinchō 金打 ceremony and begin training.
Suggested time: around 10 minutes.
Narashi-Geiko 慣らし稽古 (Repetition training)
In former times, practice began at daybreak with ippon-nuki 一本抜き (a general term for simple techniques performed without footwork), suburi 素振り (swing drills), and Doran-geiko  動乱稽古 (For example, striking standing trees) as the warm-up. After that, everyone would gather on the engawa 縁側 (veranda) or open the low windows of the dojo and perform ken-za-zen facing the morning sun. Then, following Kinchō, full training commenced.
Accordingly, if you wish to follow the formal precedent, it is best to adopt this sequence. In ordinary sessions, however, we usually conduct Narashi-Geiko as a warm-up immediately after Kinchō.
Because we prepare a monthly training menu, begin with a set of draws called narashi-geiko in accordance with that week’s plan. This method consists of repeating the draw without detailed explanations or fine corrections. Provide some explanation for absolute beginners and trial participants. If the group is large enough, it is fine to divide veterans and new members. Where possible, perform about 10-20 repetitions per technique.
If possible, then move on to the following seated forms to create good balance:
1. Ippon-nuki 一本抜き (single-draw)
2. Za-hō 坐法 Okigatana 置刀 (seated form: placing the sword)
3. Za-hō 坐法 Kodachi Battōjutsu 小太刀抜刀術 (seated form: short-sword drawing)
Aim for about 10 minutes per item.
Suggested time: total of roughly 30–40 minutes.
Doran-keiko 動乱稽古 (Moving Drills) and Specialized Training
Repeating the same technique while traveling back and forth the floor, or performing suburi while moving, is called doran-keiko (moving drills). Tenshinryu also preserves many distinctive training methods of its own (including test cutting), and these are consolidated into the monthly training menu.
These items are generally high in intensity and foster the minimum levels of conditioning and strength required in Tenshinryu. In practice, a Tenshinryu swordsman is expected to possess physical capacities on par with top athletes. While you must avoid overexertion, run these as appropriate as training to thoroughly condition the body.
Suggested time: total of about 30–40 minutes.
Instructional Talk
In the modern day there are not many opportunities to learn the Bushido that people of former times were simply expected to know, nor its history and cultural background. The same is true of Tenshinryu's thought and philosophy, as well as basic knowledge of arms and related subjects. Ishii Sensei often taught Tenshin Sensei various things not only during practice but afterward too, over coffee at a café (treat from Ishii Sensei).
We try to weave such topics into technical instruction as occasions arise, but that alone is not enough, so we also set aside time during practice to convey them from time to time. Ideally, we would devote about 10–20 minutes to this in each session.
Suggested time: about 10–20 minutes.
Other
During the time allotted for doran-geiko or specialized training, you may substitute in kenjutsu or tonomono 外物 (material from outside the core curriculum). If time allows, after doran-geiko and specialized training—and, if possible, following the instructional talk—run a kenjutsu or tonomono segment.
Suggested time: total of about 30–40 minutes.
Main Themes
Depending on the available time, work on Za-hō (Okigatana, Kodachi), standing battōjutsu, kenjutsu, and other items. Ideally, cover as many items as possible; if that is difficult due to training time or stamina, select a few to focus on. You may also divide participants into groups according to level.
Suggested time: about 15–30 minutes per theme.
End of Practice
At the end, have everyone form ranks and sit. If time is short, you may omit this and finish with a standing bow instead.
Give closing remarks to wrap up. Convey any announcements and share brief reflections on the day’s training. As at the start of practice, this calls for a touch of wit and engaging delivery.
Then conclude the session with Kinchō or with zarei 坐礼 (seated bow).
Cleanup and Breakdown
Clean up quickly and clear out.
Give directions so that students help as much as possible with cleaning and restoring the space to its original condition. It is also good to ask for help with the instructors' own cleanup.
As people are leaving, try to say a quick word to each student whenever possible. The accumulation of small interactions is the key to building trust.
