# Principles and Fundamentals of Instruction

﻿Principles and Fundamentals
of Instruction
Kuwami Masakumo
________________
Introduction
One of the current challenges facing Tenshinryu is the cultivation of instructors and the establishment of a robust instructional framework. It is true that the recent COVID-19 pandemic acted as a major brake on our activities; yet, given that our systems and mechanisms have not kept pace, we can also view this as a turning point for building manuals and institutional structures.
Public recognition of Tenshinryu has outstripped the actual growth in student numbers. At the same time, our preparations to receive larger numbers have not caught up either. Expansion without substance inevitably leads to stall and collapse. Up to now, while making every effort to promote the school, we have worked just as hard to ensure that growth does not spin out of control.
There is a common claim that traditional martial arts are fundamentally ill-suited to group instruction.
In Tenshinryu Hyoho, practice was originally organized in units of five, called kumi 組. Multiple kumi would also train together in joint sessions; even so, the samurai of the Shirin-dan received instruction as an official duty — in effect, professional military training. Accordingly, a high level of performance was demanded, backed by ample practice time, firm resolve, appropriate compensation, and concrete duties.
Because the system was not designed on the premise of teaching unspecified members of the general public as we do today, it is true in that sense that it does not lend itself to group instruction, and that the original curriculum is not directly suited to modern students as-is.
Yet if we return to fundamentals, warfare — where many samurai gathered — was, willy-nilly, group combat. In martial arts circles, talk of group combat tends to drift toward solo kata on how to fight against multiple opponents. But for samurai, who were soldiers, it was just as important to know how to lead a group or how to operate within one.
A person who lacks the art of rightly leading and guiding a group can hardly be said to possess the qualities of a commander; and one who cannot grasp and act upon the commander’s orders and tactics is a burden on the battlefield.
This is the difference between a samurai and a mere martial artist. While most martial arts are ultimately personal pursuits (including those practiced by samurai and other social classes), Ishii Sensei repeatedly told Tenshin Sensei that Tenshinryu is unlike other schools precisely because it exists for the public good beyond the individual, sometimes envisions warfare, and, above all, encompasses the commander’s hyoho. Our teachings explicitly include what is called the rank of commander (Jinmaku-hō, the ‘command-tent’ teachings and training), because a samurai must fulfill roles suited to the mission and to the situation: at times serving as a commander, and at other times disguising oneself as a traveling performer or even as a blind person or a person with a physical disability (historical terms once used for such disguises; these labels are now considered offensive).
We must not lose sight of, or forget, the fact that samurai possessed the nature of fighting men, and we must not swallow whole the generalization that ‘classical martial arts are unsuited to group instruction.’
Of course, simply copying the training system of former times will not enable group instruction. In any case, fully reproducing historical training is not feasible in our era. With respect to group instruction too, we should adapt our teaching to the circumstances as needed. To declare that classical arts are unfit for group teaching while neglecting such modern adjustments is nothing more than a lack of will, a lack of ability to do so, or an excuse for the sad reality that too few people gather in the first place.
If we are serious about passing Tenshinryu to future generations, we must keep taking on these difficult challenges.
Naturally, group instruction has its own peculiar difficulties that differ from one-on-one or small-group teaching.
Even among animals, one-on-one or small-group guidance occurs; this is possible at the level of instinct. For example, with parrots, parent birds teach their chicks how to fly, and siblings sometimes help teach as well. Even without an explicit theory of instruction, this usually happens naturally.
By contrast, I have never seen animals conduct group instruction. In other words, group instruction is a highly civilized, advanced activity made possible by the human acquisition of sophisticated intelligence and language.
Because the numbers are greater, information in group instruction saturates easily and situations change rapidly; it demands precisely the mindset of a commander maneuvering troops.
This is, in the truest sense, training in hyoho.
In Tenshinryu we aim, first, for 100 students per dojo. To achieve that, excellent instructors are indispensable — not merely excellent instructors, but instructors who can demonstrate excellent group-instruction capability.
This volume summarizes, in an accessible way, the principles and fundamentals of group instruction in martial arts.
As for my own background: from fourth grade in elementary school I served in the Shorinji Kempo youth division as vice-captain and later captain, organizing the class until the teacher arrived, forming ranks, leading calisthenics, and at times even teaching basics and moving drills. After graduating high school I attended the Japan Shorinji Budo College (Busen), where I studied dojo management and instructional theory as an instructor-in-training, and afterward I was involved in dojo operations and teaching. Since my time at Busen I have studied the mindset of instructors through many books, and I continue to study to this day.
In practice, you will need to run sessions on the floor from here on out and accumulate experience, but it is unrealistic to expect everyone to read the vast number of books I have read.
Nor is it easy to carve out time to study leadership theory in a classroom format over a long period, as we did at Busen.
For these reasons, I decided to share with you a synthesis of my experience and the knowledge I have gained to date.
Simply reading will not make you able; but charging ahead without knowledge makes it hard to recognize problems and points for improvement, and progress does not come easily. The most efficient — and, at present, the most feasible — approach to cultivating instructors is to begin by acquiring correct knowledge, then conduct on-site practicum and make incremental corrections over time.
On the Importance of Manuals
In recent years, the very idea of a manual has often been met with reluctance. In many countries, people joke about or even disparage so-called ‘manual people’ who only act by the book. This sentiment has circulated for decades and, with the rise of generations that sought to break from established values, attitudes in many places have shifted away from manual absolutism. Today, even where people acknowledge manuals as important, an anti-manual mindset has also become widespread.
In Tenshinryu as well, our instructional system did not function properly for a long time. The highly individual transmission between Ishii Sensei and Tenshin Sensei, combined with Tenshin Sensei’s unique challenges in communication, meant that the original methods of teaching were long neglected.
As a result, many senior students came to feel as if Tenshinryu and manuals mixed like oil and water. In fact, manuals did exist in former times. Even though the teachings on pedagogy we received from Tenshin Sensei are sometimes fragmentary, their overall volume is immense; if compiled, they would allow us to teach almost exactly as training was actually conducted in the past.
Of course, in modern training we cannot simply conduct everything as it was. We must make appropriate adjustments so that instruction reflects the on-the-ground reality of each dojo.
Our current efforts to create timetables and training menus are one such attempt. We have already published Miden 身伝 (Kurai 位), Tachiai Battojyutsu 12 Kata 勢法, and Tenshinryu Foundationsand Principles1. The positive impact of making things clear in text and codifying them into manuals has been unmistakable.
Some worry that standardization produces uniformity. Yet it is not as if the non-standard, highly individual instruction of Tenshin Sensei consistently produced outstanding students; in fact, nearly all students quit relatively early. If we are to transmit Tenshinryu correctly from here on, the manualization of instruction is unavoidable, and it is an urgent, critical task.
Since the Meiji Restoration, martial skills were transformed into modern budo and organized into nationwide federations. These organizations excelled not only in administration, but also in the manualization of instruction and training. That is exceptionally effective for guaranteeing a minimum standard of quality.
There are countless examples of success built on excellent manuals: McDonald’s, Starbucks (which is often said to have no ‘customer-service manual,’ yet in practice codifies many behaviors), and more. Conversely, there is no end to organizations and companies that have run aground for failing to systematize their operations. Manuals matter that much. If superior instruction and management could be delivered without manuals, that would be ideal. But what would happen if we asked every student and trial participant to rate their instructor’s teaching and management? Given student retention and the conversion rate from trials to enrollment, the results would be obvious.
Especially after the pandemic, I believe natural selection has pushed many fields worldwide toward greater professionalism.
For example, fitness gyms that people can use on their own schedule, without being subject to others’ convenience, and that offer rich facilities have recovered from the pandemic slump and reached all-time highs. By contrast, the number of budo practitioners in Japan has declined significantly (it has halved between 2010 and 2020). Other sports have not seen declines on that scale.
Budo is, at present, a shrinking industry. There are many causes, but one major factor is insufficient promotion. Another is the trap of amateurism. Classical arts since the Meiji era, and the budo founded thereafter, have largely been sustained by volunteer spirit and amateurism. In a world with limitless entertainment options, however, volunteerism and amateurism have come to be recognized as direct causes of cultural underdevelopment and insufficiency, and as serious errors that cannot be overcome.
Even so, just as many things once thought to be in decline have revived, success ultimately comes down to method. If we truly embody Lord Munenori’s katsujinken (the life-giving sword) and apply Tenshinryu as a living art rather than an empty shell, we will surely achieve great success and carry the traditional martial arts into a new era with vitality.
What Leaders Need Most
The first thing I want you to understand is that what a leader truly needs is not experience, knowledge, or ability, but a sense of mission and conviction. Most people assume the opposite: that their own experience, knowledge, and ability are paramount. Of course, that does not mean experience, knowledge, and ability are unnecessary.
The point here is one of relative importance. It may sound commonplace, but it matters greatly, because a leader who lacks a sense of mission and conviction almost never exhibits excellent leadership.
Why is that?
Because a leader is typically the head of a group with a purpose. Without a clear sense of mission toward that purpose, conviction does not arise, and under such a leader people cannot unite and strive for that purpose.
Even a person endowed with rich experience, solid knowledge, and high ability does not automatically manifest leadership.
History shows how many leaders have been young. To name just a few off the top of my head:
* Kondō Isami organized the Shinsengumi at age 29.
* Yoshida Shōin opened Shōka Sonjuku at 27 and was executed at 29.
* Morita Akio co-founded the predecessor to Sony (Tokyo Tsushin Kogyo) with Ibuka Masaru at 25.
* Neil Armstrong became the first human to set foot on the Moon at 39.
* Matsushita Konosuke founded Matsushita Electric at 24.
* Steve Jobs founded Apple at 21.
* Son Masayoshi launched his first venture at 23 and founded SoftBank the following year.
* Tokugawa Ieyasu fought his first battle at 16.
Some ages may be off by a bit, but the point stands.
There is a persistent image that the top, the instructor, the leader is an elder; I had that impression myself. In reality, that is not necessarily the case.
Did these people succeed because, from youth, they possessed extraordinary experience, flawless knowledge, and towering ability? They may have been above average, but not necessarily exceptional. If you compare someone in their 20s and someone in their 60s, the gap in sheer experience should be overwhelming.
In other words, those who came before us have proved that even if you are not yet fully formed, you can still lead and bring people with you.
Let me say it again: what matters most for a leader is a sense of mission and conviction.
Consider an even younger setting: nationwide-caliber high school clubs. Captains lead because they exercise leadership. They are not superhuman geniuses laden with decades of experience and encyclopedic knowledge. From this, it is clear that neither age nor experience justifies a lack of leadership.
As this example shows, many biases are deeply programmed into us. It is a kind of conditioning.
Natsume Sōseki wrote in Gubijinsō: 'If one does not intend to do something, one cannot even set upright the chopsticks lying on their side.' One more thing is also true: if we do not believe we can do it, we will not even attempt to set those chopsticks upright. Humans built airplanes because they believed they could fly; we went to the Moon because we believed we could go.
If you decide you cannot and never begin, you will not learn to do it.
Musha no Koji Saneatsu wrote this poem I saw on the dojo wall when I was in grade school:
'You can do it if you mean it.
If you think you cannot, you cannot.
If you think you can, you can.
Keep a positive mind to the end;
believe you can do what can be done;
and may you advance forever.
You can. You can. You will surely do it.'
This, too, is a kind of bias etched deep into my brain. In Japan we call harmful conditioning 'brainwashing' when we speak of negative cases like Aum Shinrikyo. What do we call the positive kind? Education.
Most of us have completed a standard course of education. When I graduated from Busen (an abbreviation for Japan Shorinji Budo College), my mentor, Sawamura Sensei, told me: the real work begins now. Until now, school, teachers, and environment have raised you. From here on, if you cannot raise yourself, you will not improve; you will decline.
To keep advancing forever, you must refuse to be satisfied with the status quo. Study daily and keep challenging yourself. You will fail at times, but unless you can even enjoy those bitter experiences as part of the flavor of life, you will not grow. If you stop growing and accept mere maintenance, age will soon harden you and turn you into the very kind of 'ossified elder' we scorned in our youth. You may think you are maintaining, but in reality you are deteriorating. Before you know it, you become the adult you once despised as foolish.
Samuel Ullman expressed the spirit of youth like this:
Youth — Samuel Ullman (original text)
Youth is not a time of life; it is a state of mind; it is not a matter of rosy cheeks, red lips and supple knees; it is a matter of the will, a quality of the imagination, a vigor of the emotions; it is the freshness of the deep springs of life.
Youth means a temperamental predominance of courage over timidity, of the appetite for adventure over the love of ease. This often exists in a man of sixty more than a boy of twenty. Nobody grows old merely by a number of years. We grow old by deserting our ideals.
Years may wrinkle the skin, but to give up enthusiasm wrinkles the soul.
Worry, fear, self-distrust bows the heart and turns the spirit back to dust.
Whether sixty or sixteen, there is in every human being's heart the lure of wonders, the unfailing childlike appetite of what's next, and the joy of the game of living. In the center of your heart and my heart there is a wireless station; so long as it receives messages of beauty, hope, cheer, courage and power from men and from the Infinite, so long are you young.
When the aerials are down, and your spirit is covered with the snows of cynicism and the ice of pessimism, then you are grown old, even at twenty; but as long as your aerials are up, to catch waves of optimism, there is hope you may die young at eighty.
Temperamentally, there are people we call 'fiery' and others who are cool and composed; innate differences exist. We often imagine that motivation or 'drive' is vague and fickle, and that success or stagnation depends on it. In fact, passion, motivation, and drive can be cultivated and maintained after the fact.
The key is understanding that this is possible and learning the techniques to do it.
Simple examples include listening to favorite music or preparing rewards for yourself. You can optimize your workspace, make results visible, start acting before motivation arrives (because action begets motivation), and build habits so you do not rely on motivation. Reading the words of those who came before, reading biographies of great figures, engaging with works you love to stoke your will, or even leaning on caffeine can be very useful.
There is no single method that suits everyone. Each person has methods that fit them best. By systematizing those methods, you can generate and sustain passion, will, and motivation consistently—and produce results consistently.

I will confess here that I was, by nature, an intensely negative person. I was timid, trapped in pessimistic thinking, unable to forget unpleasant events or failures from the past, and once a negative emotion was stirred up, I would drag it around for a long time. Through many years of training, however, I learned to settle my mind, to suppress and control unnecessary negative emotions, and to lift myself up.
First, I identify favorite music, manga, novels, anime, dramas, films, and comedy, and I take in what I need according to the moment. When I recall something negative, encounter something negative, or feel an anxiety full of unknowns that thinking cannot resolve, I make a point of not staring it in the face. Instead, I switch tasks or watch/read the favorites mentioned above. Of course, spending time with others and training are also effective. Sitting in meditation as needed to calm and deepen the breath is helpful, and taking a bath or eating something you like can also be useful.
In any case, the key is not to rely on compensatory reactions. Otherwise it becomes mere escape, or turns into binge drinking and overeating. Keep an emotional distance from the negative factor, and deal with whatever needs doing—if anything does—calmly and matter-of-factly. Buddhist philosophy also played an extremely important role for me, but I will leave that aside here.
It took more than ten years for this training to bear general fruit, and even now I continue to practice it every day.
There is no single method that suits everyone. Each person has methods that fit them best. By systematizing those methods, you can generate and sustain passion, will, and motivation consistently—and produce results consistently.
Leaders Spread the Flame
During my days at Busen, my mentor Sawamura Sensei once asked us:
'What is the single most important quality in an instructor?'
Everyone answered with things like leadership, technique, strength — all of which were wrong.
As noted earlier, a leader above all needs a sense of mission and conviction. Yet Sawamura Sensei gave a different answer that day.
He said: 'It is energy.'
He went on:
'The Founder was a terrible speaker. But he was passionate and full of energy — even though he was an old man with a heart condition!? That is the most important thing!'
The instructional talks of Sō Doshin Sensei, the founder of Shorinji Kempo, were notorious: they were exceedingly long, their thrust and conclusions were unclear, and he would often digress or wander.I have listened to many recordings, and frankly they can be hard to sit through. But they were powerful. Simply listening, you felt something that stirred your spirit and made you feel more alive. Sawamura Sensei called that something 'energy.'
Of course, energy alone is not enough — but a leader without energy is no leader. Do not misunderstand: by energy I do not mean being loud.
This is where the sense of mission and conviction first mentioned above become essential.
True energy is what arises from that mission and conviction.
Energy certainly affects others for the better. Given the nature of a tradition, however, a leader’s energy must have direction; if it is merely noisy, it can actually sap the motivation of those around you.
Even a quiet person, if endowed with mission and conviction, shows that energy as intensity in the eyes. It manifests as an aura from the body.
This total energy becomes a blaze that spreads — it ignites other people.
Naturally, it may have little effect on those with dull sensibility, or on the stubborn who have unconsciously decided, 'I am me; I won’t be influenced by others.' Even so, humans are creatures who synchronize without realizing it; such energy reaches deep into the heart.
Possessing Influence
An essential condition for an excellent instructor is to possess influence. Simply being given a title does not produce leadership. Influence over others consists of two elements: the conviction noted earlier, and charisma. Brisk vitality is one form of charisma; needless to say, attractive appearance is another. A good voice, skillful speaking, and intelligence are also forms of charisma. In martial arts, technical finesse, beauty of movement, and sheer strength translate directly into charisma.
For Tenshinryu, the last of these is the most important. Yet today a single-point breakthrough seldom suffices. In a high-information society, everything tends to be refined.
A generation ago, many people would freeze up when a camera or microphone was pointed at them. Now there are plenty of amateurs who can say clever or funny things on TV. Many people learn impressive magic or polished dance routines from knowledge found online. Reaching a level that rivals professionals has become relatively easy.
Of course, compared to true professionals there remains a large gap; but to the general public, that gap can be hard to perceive. As a result, the contest now is how broadly you can raise your points of appeal across multiple dimensions.
In the hit manga Dragon Ball, the Saiyan race includes the legendary Super Saiyan said to appear once in a thousand years. The Saiyan protagonist reaches that state through long training and fierce battles, but as the series continues, other Saiyans (including human–Saiyan hybrids) also awaken as Super Saiyans. One character quips, “It’s like a bargain sale on Super Saiyans!”
This is not just fiction. In artistic gymnastics, difficulty once topped out at C, and anything beyond was dubbed “Ultra C,” set apart as extraordinary. Over time, more and more athletes mastered such ultra-difficult elements, which eventually became mere connecting skills. Today the men’s code ranges from A to I, and the women’s from A to J. When exceptional feats propagate, they eventually become commonplace.
In short, what once worked by relying on a single strength or distinctive trait no longer suffices in today’s world.
People with broad influence used to be called “charismatic.” Unless Tenshinryu produces such charismatic leaders, we will be swallowed by the larger tide and disappear.
Negative Emotions Spread Easily
As you know, just as positive emotions spread, negative emotions also infect others — and they do so more powerfully. Our primitive brains are wired so that sharing negative information boosts group survival, whereas sharing good news does not directly raise survival odds.
Therefore, to let positive feelings — which spread less readily — catch and spread, you need a fierce, roaring flame.
Especially when students arrive tired from work, school, or family life, the leader must carry a blaze strong enough to blow away that negativity and deliberately let it catch in those around them.
When turnout is small, it is a moment when an instructor’s motivation is hard to maintain; sometimes no one comes at all. A temporary dip in drive is only human. But if you recognize that fact, then all the more you must kindle your teaching fire and provide a worthy session; otherwise you cannot face the students who did make the effort to come.
Precisely because the group is small, treat it as a chance for something special. Yes, it is important to aim for steady, even instruction at all times. But in low-energy, small-group settings, you need to stoke your own heat enough just to break even. If an instructor loses the passion for teaching, that loss will inevitably spread to the students.
If no one comes, regard it as a perfect opportunity for your own training and throw yourself into solo practice. As noted above, will and motivation do not always arise on their own. If you cannot generate and sustain them yourself, you cannot hope to live a long life that is joyful, flavorful, and continually changing and improving; before you know it, your inner engine will be sooted up and stalled, and your heart will feel like a pile of cold ashes.
Turn Even Negatives Into Positives
A leader walks into the dojo, mumbles a greeting in a small voice, or fails to greet at all. They sigh often and rasp, "I’m tired."
Who would want instruction from such a negative leader? In fact, even as an ordinary student, being near them would not feel good.
You do not need to shout, but a proper leader’s greeting should carry this message: "From here I will instruct you so you can have the best possible time improving your Tenshinryu!" You must always put your heart’s message into your words, your gaze, and your aura.
Do this, and even a simple greeting becomes a gem.
Of course leaders get tired. Sometimes negative remarks are unavoidable. But a true leader turns negatives into positives.
Work is rough, training is exhausting... But precisely because of that, vent your work fatigue and stress through practice, and vent your practice fatigue and stress through practice! Turn it into cheerful grumbling.
When uttered brightly, even such negative statements do not spread a minus mood. Takizawa Sensei is good at this. He often complains about his workplace, yet never makes it feel heavy. As a result people say, "You always seem to be having fun," "You look like you have no worries," and he snaps back, "I do struggle, and I do have worries!" (Even this retort is not negative.) In other words, rephrased, what they are saying is: "It feels good to be around you."
What Is the World's Best Martial Arts Instructor?
We in Tenshinryu must become the world's best martial arts instructors from here on. So what, exactly, is an excellent martial arts instructor?
Once again, the definition is not technique, strength, or even coaching skill.
'It doesn’t sell because it’s delicious; the food that sells is the delicious food.'
This is the title of a book by Saizeriya (It is Japan's most famous Italian restaurant chain, known for tasty food at low prices. It has a total of 1569 locations,1038 in Japan and 531 overseas, as of 2024) founder Yasuhiko Shogaki — an aggressively provocative line.
Even if you use the finest ingredients, have the world’s best chef, the grandest décor, and the most exquisite tableware, if there are zero customers, it is not the world’s best restaurant.
Likewise, even if someone has the world’s most advanced technique and strength, and on top of that flawless coaching ability, if they have not a single student, they are not the world’s best martial arts instructor.
First and foremost, they must attract many people and keep them coming back. At the same time, students should improve in proportion to training, with minimal dependence on innate aptitude or prior experience; the school should produce many excellent martial artists and instructors; and day-to-day training satisfaction should be consistently high.
The person who can make all of this happen is the world’s best martial arts instructor.
There are many people who are outstanding as martial artists: superb technique, sheer strength, beautiful demonstrations, clear logic. But even if one excels as a martial artist, that does not automatically make one an excellent instructor.
'A great player is not necessarily a great manager.'
This is a common saying in the world of sports: a player with outstanding results is not necessarily outstanding as a coach.
In the workplace, too, it is common sense that someone superb on the front line may not be equally superb in management. For decades we have heard the claim that Japanese firms promote high performers into management without assessing suitability, thereby hobbling the organization (a story, in fact, that is common in Europe and America as well).
All of this shows that a superior martial artist who simply starts teaching does not thereby become a superior instructor.
It is hard enough to produce excellent martial artists; producing excellent instructors on top of that is harder still. Yet, as noted above, today excellence spreads and becomes commonplace; a narrow edge no longer suffices to survive. We must therefore tackle these two difficult tasks seriously and make them real, or we have no future.
Moreover, the qualifications for being an instructor do not lie on the simple extension of one’s own martial training. This book presents an instructor’s theory precisely from that perspective.
The Role of the Instructor
An instructor’s purpose, of course, is to help students improve. Yet few people can keep their attention on what is actually required to make that happen. The key is to maximize the quality of each and every practice session.
The basic premise is that through practice, techniques are refined and one advances. That is why we devote ourselves to training. The same holds for instruction: by always striving for better, aiming for an ideal we have yet to reach, we can raise training quality every time. Simply conducting classes on autopilot will not improve one’s teaching. Like technique, instructional skill is an ability that can be acquired through steady effort. And, just like technique, it is not so easy that you will master it merely by being taught.
You must accumulate experience, set concrete goals for each session, review the content after practice, and reflect on shortcomings and points for improvement in your own teaching, then make corrections. When something troubles you, it is important to consult seniors for their views, and, when appropriate, to ask students directly for feedback with a humble attitude.
Grasping the Whole
An instructor must always have a grasp of the whole. The most foolish leader is one who makes judgments without obtaining the necessary information. That is no different from leaving choices and decisions to the roll of a die, and it does nothing to expand your options. If you choose from under-informed options where no correct answer exists, you will of course fail to teach well.
To grasp the whole, put another way, is to see how fully you can gather information about the whole.
This is the first priority in practical instruction. Only on that basis do you apply ingenuity and make judgments. If you proceed on missing or mistaken information, things will generally go well only by accident. After experiencing a session that happened to go well by chance, you may feel confident—but that is not an instructor’s ability, it is merely luck. In principle, each practice should pair two outcomes—training satisfaction and improvement—so students spend the time in the best possible way.
Here we focus on grasping a single practice session. In turn, consistently grasping each session leads to understanding and mastering the school’s overall state, which enables you to run the tradition rightly and better.
Even within “grasping the whole,” there are several types, depending on the nature of the information involved.
Grasping the Schedule (Time Management)
Time is numerical, so it is the clearest, most visible domain to grasp.
Always keep track of the total practice time, the current time, and the time remaining. At a minimum, print the timetable on paper and check your teaching against it. Of course, in real practice it is not always best to allocate time exactly as written. You must adjust to the group’s level and the degree of focus in the room to reach an optimal distribution. But if you lack a baseline allocation and run practice only by feel, sessions will skew, and you will keep repeating unstable classes that depend on luck. When the content is unstable, steady improvement cannot be expected.
On breaks
Breaks are vital to prevent injury and heat illness. Decide on their timing, frequency, and length, and insert them appropriately. Too many breaks disrupt concentration. Human attention is often said to top out around twenty minutes, but we rarely maintain that level of focus; and we already pause for quick hydration when switching items. There is no need to break excessively.
One practical approach is to schedule breaks quantitatively—e.g., once every 40 minutes, or between blocks on the menu. This is the more common instructional style. That said, with menus still under development and content unstable in some places, strict scheduling can be difficult.
Breaks as a restart / reset
Breaks can also be used to restart a session that has begun to sag. At Tenshinryu—especially in headquarters training—we used this fairly often. When students felt strong weariness or aversion toward Tenshin Sensei’s ongoing instruction, inserting a break helped them refresh.
In other words, it’s a reset; if practice is proceeding at a good tempo, you may not need such breaks.
That said, Tenshinryu practice uses many unique terms, and beyond the technical content there is a great deal of surrounding information—placing a heavy load on the brain. Even without physical fatigue, mental fatigue and loss of concentration are common sights on the floor. Short breaks are therefore important for recovering from this kind of cognitive fatigue.
Timing your breaks
Instructors often (unconsciously) call a break right when a late-arriving student steps in, because the rhythm was disturbed by greetings and they want a restart. For the latecomer, though, a break the moment they begin is deflating.
Ask yourself: is a break truly necessary at that exact moment? If this happens often, it has become a habit of ‘throwing in a break.’
When a break really is needed at such a time, adjust with hospitality: for example, ‘You just arrived—please get your body moving with a few quick single-draw reps,’ or give a brief update—‘Here’s what we were just doing…’ / ‘We did a brush-up on…’—or do those quick reps together. Always respond flexibly to the person’s level and situation. Keep asking, ‘How would I feel in their place?’ The worst approach is neglect—leaving them unattended without any consideration. Few instructors do this well, but it is essential to act with omotenashi—true hospitality.
Know the purpose of each break
This is chiefly for the instructor’s own clarity: Are you breaking to prevent heat injury or avoid strains? Simply for fatigue recovery? Or to reset the room? If you do not grasp your purpose, you will mistime breaks or insert unnecessary ones—and the quality of practice will suffer.
Avoid Making Practice Monotonous or Long-winded
Many instructors tend to run practice at a single tempo. Human attention scatters more easily than we think; when training is monotonous or redundant, people quickly grow bored and lose focus. So vary the rhythm like music, and insert shifts like a film.
If you are repeating reps, minimize commentary and keep things moving without interruption. If the aim is to correct movement or learn a sequence, slow down and work deliberately. Near the end, pick up the pace for a final push. In short, change the tempo.
After solo drills, switch to paired work; add traveling drills; if people seem tired, use a quiz-style Q&A or spend time on explanation; have individuals or pairs perform while the group observes—switch modes as needed.
Aim for training that stays engaging and taut with focus.
Martial artists often say practice is not “entertainment.”
entertainment — (dictionary meanings)
* hospitality; reception of guests
* enjoyment, pleasure, or diversion received from something
* entertainment as a field; show business
* a staged entertainment; a performance or show
In this broader sense, practice does have an entertainment aspect. And for modern people—who can enjoy countless diversions with a few taps on a smartphone—it is hard to spend fulfilling time in an environment that lacks thoughtful consideration. We are presenters of the finest “material,” Tenshinryu.
Companies spend heavily on advertising to convey the excellence of their products and services. No matter how good something is, if we lack the zeal to communicate it and adopt an attitude of “only those who get it need get it,” unpopularity and decline are only natural.
Command of the Training Floor
It is the instructor’s duty to govern the dojo space.
The instructor must grasp the situation and condition of every participant, keep a constant watch, and, when necessary, attend not only to each student’s practice but also to observers and other facility users.
Sense the overall atmosphere of the session and adjust instruction as needed. Know each student’s level and current state, keep an eye on them, and tailor what you teach accordingly. Conveying advanced content to someone who still struggles with basic procedure will not aid progress; it will confuse them and set them back. At times, however, adding an accent to practice by assigning a slightly harder task is useful. If someone is at a loss and cannot move, demonstrate and work alongside them yourself, or ask a senior to assist.
It is also essential to eliminate hazards during practice. You must constantly watch the surrounding movement and direct traffic. Of course, each practitioner bears responsibility as well, but the instructor is the person in charge on the floor, and ensuring safety is a vital role.
If someone is carelessly too close to a wall, if students are too near each other, or if bags are placed nearby, spot it quickly and instruct them to change position. In groups, a frequent near-miss is nearly stabbing the person behind with a sword. Make sure not only you but every student understands this risk; have them stay mindful of what’s behind them during practice, and foster an environment in which students can sense danger signs and warn one another.
On Positioning and Lineup
It is the instructor’s role to direct students’ lineup and positioning. The relative placement of students has a significant impact on practice.
When forming a circle, avoid seating/standing beginners next to one another.
Beginners often cannot judge where to place themselves and tend to act by watching others; they also seek reassurance by staying near other beginners. By contrast, more experienced students can choose their spots smoothly, so when numbers are large, novices naturally clump together.
When forming a circle, address the group: “Please don’t cluster beginners together—try to stand next to someone who looks skilled,” and “Being near the skilled helps you improve faster!”
If two beginners end up side by side, immediately instruct an intermediate or advanced student to step between them.
The aim is partly to ensure someone can correct them when needed, and even otherwise they can glance sideways at a model. This kind of indirect guidance is crucial in group instruction.
When I give detailed positional directions during practice, it is always intentional. If a pairing yields no positive effect, I keep changing it. We owe practitioners the utmost consideration. The attitude “those who can will, those who can’t won’t” leaves progress to innate talent alone.
At times, also consider interpersonal dynamics—who gets along with whom—when arranging positions.
You may, when appropriate, group students by level (beginner, intermediate, advanced).
Of course an instructor must focus on teaching. But the role is to be 100% focused on instruction, 100% on situational awareness, and, on top of that, to bring 100% commitment to one’s own practice—all at once.
An excellent instructor is, in this sense, a ‘superhuman’; making this possible requires constant ingenuity and effort. Yet none of this is innate—these are abilities anyone can learn and acquire.

Metsuke 目付 (The Instructor’s Eye)
For an instructor to judge rightly, it is vital to keep eyes on the whole at all times.
This is precisely metsuke 目付: kan no me 観の眼, the eye that takes in the whole; shin no me 心の眼, the mind’s eye that fixes on particulars; and happō-moku 八方目, “eyes in eight directions” that attend to what lies beyond the front. The metsuke of hyoho is not only for combat—it is to be used always.
Needless to say, during instruction you must employ these modes of metsuke to read the situation; otherwise you cannot guide practice well. He who controls information controls the fight. As Sunzi says: “Know the enemy and know yourself; in a hundred battles you will not be imperiled. Know yourself but not the enemy, and you win one and lose one. Know neither the enemy nor yourself, and every battle puts you in danger.”
We teach many kinds of metsuke precisely to cultivate the way of seeing. Gazing blankly, looking without understanding, and observing with comprehension are all “looking,” yet what you gain from them differs as heaven from earth.
In the same way, an instructor must constantly scan the surroundings. With years of practice this grows into keigan 慧眼—a discerning eye that gathers ever more, and ever deeper, information.
Reject the Easy and Slapdash; Aim for Thoroughness
Abandon the mindset of “This will do.” If you cannot say “This is the best,” your thinking will drift toward the path of least resistance, and your teaching will become ad hoc and unplanned—its only “purpose” being to fritter away the remaining practice time without care or design.
That attitude will inevitably spread to the students and sap the group’s will to train. Yet you cannot gain the conviction to say “This is the best” unless you have a firm grasp of the present situation. In other words, a leader who does not comprehend the whole tends to avoid planning and to act on the spur of the moment.
Of course, in Tenshinryu right now our resources are limited, while tasks pile up—forcing us to make extremely difficult choices. What is required here is sound judgment.
If you shy away from that struggle, you end up teaching based only on your personal “current craze,” as Tenshin Sensei sometimes did in the absence of planning. In his case the primary aim of instruction was the transmission of the tradition rather than the students’ improvement, so such tendencies may have been understandable. But no matter how earnestly we pursue transmission, if we do not cultivate students, then neither correct understanding nor correct form nor the essence itself will be handed down; only a hollow shell will remain.
Give Clear, Explicit Instructions
In Japanese, the word 「指導 Shidō (Instruction)」 literally means “to point (指) with the finger (指) and lead (導).” Writing “to point (指 and 示) with the finger (指)” as a word gives 「指示 Shiji (directions / directive)」. In other words, 「指示」 is the very substance of 「指導」, and nothing is farther from real progress than training without clear, explicit directions from the instructor.
What are we doing?
How are we doing it?
By stating the content and purpose of practice, students know exactly where to place their attention. Of course, some students who don’t yet understand how to learn will ignore instructions and train however they please, drifting away from improvement. That lack of learning ability is a serious issue, and as a school we must raise students’ capacity to learn—but that is no excuse for an instructor to withhold instructions.
If you hold back because students don’t readily listen, that is negligence and a lack of responsibility.
You must present both the main objective for this particular session and, considering individual levels, the specific points each person should focus on.
Then confirm that your directions were actually understood. In practice, people often fail to hear instructions. If you feel satisfied merely by having given them and miss those who didn’t catch them, your guidance won’t help.
Naturally, students must also train themselves to direct attention immediately when the instructor gives instructions. In combat especially, missing information can be a matter of life and death.
Project Your Voice Above All
As noted earlier, being energetic and being noisy are not the same.
That said, one of the biggest determinants of an instructor’s effectiveness is vocal volume. If your voice is quiet, students can’t catch your directions or explanations, time is wasted, focus breaks, and you appear uncertain—your pull as a leader drops sharply.
When confidence falters, the voice tends to shrink. Even if it feels like a bluff, start by projecting a strong voice.
Vocal power is a key capacity for a samurai and is critically important in martial arts.
Delivery also matters. Record your teaching on video and review it: volume, enunciation, pacing, and tone. Train these as needed.
There’s a well-known finding called “Mehrabian’s rule,” which examined the relative impact of different information channels:
* Verbal (words): 7%
* Vocal (tone/voice): 38%
* Visual (facial/body cues): 55%
Put simply: voice quality—tone, pace, and so on—often influences listeners more than the literal content. No matter how good your material or how correct your explanation, if you neglect your voice and delivery, your message won’t land.
Maintain a Polished Appearance
Inevitably, you must also pay attention to how you look. The correlation between likeability and outward factors such as cleanliness, hairstyle, and dress goes without saying.
Instructors in particular serve as the face of Tenshinryu. Of course, this is true not only for instructors—every student, like it or not, carries the Tenshinryu signboard. It is the same as how, abroad, any Japanese person is seen as representing Japan, or how visitors to Japan are judged as representatives of their own countries by their conduct.
All the more because an instructor’s role is public, you will also meet people of standing more often. In that sense, you must maintain at least basic grooming not only in the dojo but in daily life as well.
Until fairly recently, neat appearance was demanded as a minimal mark of dignity for working adults, but that ethos has faded with diversifying values.
Precisely for that reason, in today’s world careful grooming sets you apart from the crowd.
Beyond that, if your table manners and other etiquette are lacking, people may conclude you are not someone worth learning from.
We live in an age that celebrates “being your natural self,” but that is like pulling a daikon out of the field and serving it to a guest with soil and bugs still on it. Humans are social animals; a lack of social polish translates directly into poor evaluations.
We are taught not to judge others by appearances, and that very lesson proves how instinctively humans do judge by appearances. Even if you believe you shouldn’t, your unconscious still forms an evaluation.
Steve Jobs is often cited here. He always wore the same black turtleneck, jeans, and sneakers, and people say he succeeded by devoting to work the energy others spend on fashion. But there are countless successes besides Steve Jobs, and they did not all follow the same method; moreover, his attire functioned as a kind of symbol—and above all, you are not Steve Jobs. When you already have enough—or too much—then subtracting is wise; subtracting while still lacking is a serious mistake.
The belief that “if our techniques are appealing, if we have real ability and strength, society will value us” is not truth but wishful thinking—and it is precisely for that reason that many classical martial arts have declined and been lost.
There is a well-known experiment reported by The Washington Post. On Friday, January 12, 2007, during the morning rush at the L’Enfant Plaza Metro arcade in Washington, D.C., world-renowned violinist and Grammy winner Joshua Bell played incognito for 43 minutes, performing six classical works—not “six Bach pieces,” but two by Bach plus Massenet, Schubert, Ponce, and a sixth likely Mendelssohn—on his 1713 Stradivarius (“Gibson ex Huberman”) valued at about $3.5 million. In that time 1,097 commuters passed; seven people stopped for a while; 27 donated, for a total of $32 and change. Three days earlier, Bell had performed to a full house at Boston’s Symphony Hall. The Post staged the event to probe how context, perception, and priorities shape what we notice: in a banal setting at an inconvenient time, would beauty register at all?
This episode illustrates how perceived value is powerfully conditioned by context—from printed posters and ticket prices to acoustics, décor, seating, attire, and relentless promotion—so that “good things” are not always recognized or rewarded without the framing that signals their worth.
In classical arts, many instructors neglect grooming, speech, and etiquette. As a result, society has often rejected people who lacked the social common sense taken for granted not long ago. Both my father and mother met and spoke with Tenshin Sensei; their assessments were very low. Outside the dojo, out of training clothes, when no technique is on display, by what should people evaluate you? Your clothing, your manner of speech, the content of your conversation, whether your etiquette and table manners are proper—what else is there to go on? And if the person you meet happens to be influential, will they offer help to someone poorly rated, give them an opportunity, or introduce them to a power broker?
In martial arts and budo circles, a great deal of time and effort is spent disparaging other schools. But from the outside, there is only one verdict: 「Onaji ana no Mujina 同じ穴の狢」—“all badgers from the same hole,” i.e., birds of a feather (in the pejorative sense). Mujina—the badger—is adorable, but sadly the phrase is used as a slur.
Unless we become exceptional, we will not distinguish ourselves from those who brought disrepute upon martial arts and budo in the first place.
Instruction Is a Service
We invite people to come and train. In a broad sense, instruction is a kind of service business. The service provided is practice—kata—or instruction itself. You could also call it hospitality in the modern sense.
Because this is culture, not a daily necessity, we must make it something worth gathering for. The old attitude of “I’m doing you the favor of teaching” no longer works. Of course, regardless of the era, it goes without saying that one should respect teachers and seniors. The rate of improvement differs greatly between those who show respect and those who do not. It is hard to learn wholeheartedly from someone you look down on.
Likewise, without respecting your students, your instruction will not be accepted. If you demand respect from others while slighting them yourself, you cannot build a good teacher–student relationship. (This holds for all human relationships.)
Enjoying practice and improving, in essence, are matters for the individual.
When you find yourself thinking at a shop, “Their service is terrible!”, take that as a mirror for us. Daily life overflows with lessons and insights for instruction.
If you try to draw all learning only from the dojo or from practice, your view becomes narrow and your thinking small.
To reiterate: the old mindset of “I’m doing you a favor by teaching you” no longer works.「將欲奪之、必固與之」—Laozi’s words: “If you wish to gain (to take), first give.” If you want honor, praise, and respect from students, you must first demonstrate instruction worthy of it. Being an instructor does not earn you automatic respect.
Teach Each Student in Earnest
A martial arts instructor once told me, “No matter how much I taught, my students never improved, so I disbanded the group and quit teaching.” He was blaming the students, but in the end that amounts to defeat as an instructor.
Teaching can indeed feel futile. Not everyone continues training. For most students, the time they can devote to practice is far from sufficient.
Even so, there is a saying: “The teacher–student bond spans three lifetimes.” It is also called a pact across three worlds—past, present, and future—signifying a bond of deep karmic ties. Such tightly knit relationships may be rare today, but at the very least the instructor should adopt that mindset: cherish each student and commit to teaching them wholeheartedly.
You may not be able to bring every student to a level that fully satisfies you. Even so, if each person can seize something and advance with every practice, and if their training helps make their life more fruitful, that contributes far more to society than producing a handful of ‘masters.’ In that case you can rightly, and with confidence, be called a fine teacher.
Balancing Confidence and Humility
In Tenshinryu, we aim to cultivate people who possess confidence without conceit and humility without self-abasement. This is not easy, because humans are poor at seeing themselves objectively.
Strictly speaking, even if no other creature matches humans at objectivity, ours is still insufficient. Assume, in most cases, that your self-assessment is mistaken. Only overwhelming, demonstrable results warrant a sound judgment of yourself. Any confidence that exists without results may, without harm, be called overconfidence.
For a Tenshinryu instructor, “results” mean: students you teach steadily improve; they recognize that improvement; their motivation stays high; their satisfaction with practice is high; and, as a consequence, they rate you highly as an instructor.
Yet because instructors are addressed as “Sensei,” even by seniors at times, praise—flattery or not—can make one conceited. Before you know it, you become an insufferable, overconfident instructor drunk on self-regard. You must guard daily against overconfidence, continue to refine both teaching and technique, and practice humility. If not, you will be left behind by the times; age will bring decline in ability and stamina; you will live off the “savings” of past training; soon students will overtake you and you will be dismissed as a teacher who only talks.
There is a simple way to succeed in any business: listen to your customers. No matter how convinced you are that you provide a good product or service, very often that conviction is only your own.
A quick way to gain a third-party perspective is to ask students—those who actually receive your instruction—for impressions rather than abstract opinions. This kind of inquiry is itself a skill. Of course you must not swallow feedback uncritically, but it is essential for surfacing issues and for calibrating your internal sense of what you’re actually doing well.
Likewise, recognizing your strengths is no easier than spotting your weaknesses. The Japanese are often described—fairly or not—as unduly self-effacing. Lack of confidence shows in one’s manner and even in results (e.g., in sport).
As noted, overconfidence is dangerous; but many people confuse humility with self-abasement, masking a lack of confidence behind a show of modesty. Timidity dampens ability.
Tenshinryu is transmitted as Hoshin (self-preservation) strategy. Rushing in without grasping your own capacity or the situation is reckless bravado, not the conduct of a true warrior. Timidity—in the sense of prudence—is indeed a vital samurai quality; but failing to know yourself or read the situation and therefore shrinking from action is mere cowardice.
Confidence raises others’ estimation of you. People respect and follow you because you know the work and results you’ve amassed. If you find yourself lacking in physical ability, technique, or knowledge, then get to work—openly and resolutely—and aim higher.
In the real world, many leaders fear failure or looking ignorant, so they paper things over, conceal shortcomings, and avoid challenges. Some “legendary” instructors will deal only with a handful of top disciples and keep all others at arm’s length. That may inflate charisma and help build a quasi-cultish organization, but Tenshinryu rejects such sham methods.
A true instructor is one who continually elevates himself, uses that stance to foster the next generation, and thereby earns their respect.
Never Skip Post-Session Reflection
The way to improve as an instructor is the same as the way to improve in training:
reflect after every session and act on what you find. Few people actually do this, even though it seems obvious. Technique and teaching may improve “naturally” during practice, but deliberately reviewing the day’s content and instruction inevitably raises results.
Why are there so many poorly rated teachers—not only in martial arts but across education? Because they do not improve. They blame students or circumstances instead of recognizing their own shortcomings.
After practice, ask yourself whether anything needs fixing: time allocation, word choice, watchfulness and consideration, the selection of techniques, the angle of your demonstrations, even how you positioned the students. When you observe an excellent instructor, study what differs from your own approach; when you see a poor example, check whether you share the same flaws.
You can do this anywhere, not just in the dojo. As Confucius said: “When you see the worthy, think to match them; when you see the unworthy, examine yourself within” (見賢思斉・見不賢而内自省).
There are always many wise people around us to emulate, and foolish ones we should not—and at times both reside within ourselves. Therefore we have abundant opportunities to learn in our everyday lives.
Saying “that person never changes” can be praise in the best sense; in the worst sense, it is hopeless. Before trying to change others, change yourself—otherwise the idea of positively influencing anyone is a fantasy. How often have you been truly influenced for the better by someone you do not respect?
Understanding Tenshinryu’s Strengths (Our “Weapons”)
Lastly—and most importantly—you yourself must know the value of Tenshinryu better than anyone. So what is that value?
Is it the tradition of more than four hundred years?
The teaching of Lord Munenori’s katsujinken 活人剣 (“life-giving sword”)?
The spirit of bushidō 武士道 handed down from the Shirindan 士林団?
The astonishingly subtle, systematic, and varied body of techniques?
Each of these is of great value. And above all, what I consider Tenshinryu’s greatest value is that it is “a place where exceptional people gather.” In fact, this was a hallmark from the very founding of the Shirindan.
Lord Munenori assembled a special Shirin unit to serve the Shinkun 神君 (Divine Lord—Tokugawa Ieyasu 徳川家康). It was a yoriai-jotai 寄合所対—a motley, non-uniform assembly—including masters from various schools; shinobi from Iga 伊賀 and Kōga 甲賀; remnants of the Takeda forces 武田軍; and battle-hardened warriors maimed in war but sure of their skill.
Each member brought strengths that covered another’s weaknesses; they rendered meritorious service; and in time Lord Munenori was appointed the first Chief Inspector (Ōmetsuke 大目付) to the Tokugawa shogunate. What began as a small band was granted the name “Shirindan Kōgan 光願 under the Ōmetsuke.”
As the Shirindan passed from its initial members to their successors, it needed an integrated, comprehensive martial system that encompassed the knowledge and skills required for its duties. Lord Munenori therefore released (permitted) Tokizawa Yahei 時沢弥平—said to have been like his own shadow—from close service and directed him to organize Tenshinryu as “a school surpassing Shinkage(-ryu).”
Even when a tradition is standardized as a “school,” individuals still have their own characters—ridges and hollows. Operating under a kumi-gashira 組頭 (unit head) in teams of five meant fulfilling duties by playing to each person’s strengths and compensating for their weaknesses.
Weakness, when mutually covered, becomes strength. Tenshinryu brings together truly distinctive individuals—and we do not flatten that individuality with uniform, homogenizing instruction. In Tenshinryu, each person makes the most of their individuality; through Tenshinryu we give living expression to the true katsujinken 活人剣 in the modern age—and we hope instructors will be exemplary models who embody this continually.
Each of us is a seed filled with inexhaustible potential. Whether that possibility blossoms—or remains a seed—depends on oneself. Unless you begin by believing in yourself, you will accomplish nothing. The word self-confidence literally means “to believe in oneself.”
