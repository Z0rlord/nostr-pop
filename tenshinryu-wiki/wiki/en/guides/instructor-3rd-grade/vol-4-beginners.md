# Teaching Guidelines for Beginners

﻿Teaching Guidelines for Beginners
Kuwami Masakumo
________________
Introduction
In this section I will focus on instruction for beginners and first-time visitors.
Teaching has long been regarded as difficult, and it must also take a form suited to the times. This is no easy task. Under the influence of Tenshin Sensei’s “monster-style” instruction and in the absence of a full teaching manual, many of you may feel as if you’re being swept away by a flood when you teach.
When attendance is high and there are beginners or trial participants among the group, things are especially prone to spin out of control. For newcomers the experience can feel unfriendly, and for other students it can create dissatisfaction—“someone is holding us back.”
Of course, raising the quality of practice is something practitioners themselves should strive for; blaming everything on the instructor betrays a lack of maturity.
Even so, from the instructor’s side the cardinal principle is not to fault the students’ shortcomings but to improve one’s own level of instruction as far as possible. Without adopting a sense of personal responsibility here, there will be no progress through reflection and improvement.
To keep this from getting long and blurring the key points, I will briefly set out some guidelines and know-how on two topics: group instruction and one-on-one instruction. Please use them for reference.
On Group Instruction
First, let’s look at group instruction—e.g., teaching with everyone seated in a circle (madoi) or lined up. This is the hardest format. Within one group you’ll have different levels and stages; if you over-cater to beginners and first-timers, practice for the others suffers. But you also cannot leave the beginners behind.
Below I explain how to balance and conduct the session.
________________
1) Make clear choices and give clear instructions
If there are no beginners/first-timers, you can often proceed by naming the technique and giving only key cautions. But when newcomers are present—people who don’t yet know the scenario, steps, or movements—they cannot do anything without help.
In broad strokes you have three options:
(i) Provide a special explanation for the beginners.
(ii) Ask another instructor, leader, or senior student to stand by them and give one-on-one guidance.
(iii) Have them copy a rough outline by sight for now.
The worst (and most common) mistake is to simply leave them alone. That leaves beginners and trial students not knowing what to do; such neglect is tantamount to abandoning instruction.
________________
(i) Provide a special explanation for the beginners
This means giving them the information they need.
“This is called 掬太刀 (Sukuidachi). The opponent goes to draw; you step diagonally forward as you draw, rotate the blade up from below, and cut upward—an eccentric technique and fairly difficult. Because you scoop upward (Sukuu 掬う—Sukui 掬い) as you cut, it’s called Sukuidachi.”
“This one is, for example, when someone grabs your chest—very close ma-ai. You draw horizontally; as the tip clears the 鯉口 (koiguchi, the mouth of the scabbard), pinch the tip lightly between the left thumb and index finger. Circle the fist back; set the blade’s side—鎬 (shinogi)—to your right flank. Release the left hand from the tip and open it on the back (峰, mune) of the blade. Spread your feet, bend your knees, and give a small thrust. Draw the sword as if opening a folding fan. Written ‘to insert 挿’ + ‘fan 扇,’ this is 挿扇 (Sugeōgi).”
What matters here is not full understanding or flawless execution, but letting them feel like they understand enough to begin. True comprehension comes only through repeated reps, repeated step-by-step explanations, and repeated corrections. But before practice, you can give them a toehold—a trigger. People fear “not knowing” (more precisely, they fear being scolded or thought foolish for not knowing). If told “just copy what you see,” some will simply stand and watch. Even a rough outline lets them start moving, however tentatively. And by dropping a term like koiguchi in the Sugeōgi example, you seed knowledge that applies beyond the immediate technique.
Sometimes you can be even simpler:
“You’re facing each other at close ma-ai, and you’re in the act of bowing. At that moment your opponent puts a hand on the hilt to draw. You rotate the sword with the scabbard (saya and all) and draw it together with the scabbard—but let’s leave the fine points aside for now and just give it a try.”
Then add a mnemonic origin:
“Picture your right arm as a fishing rod, the blade as the line, the tip as the bait, and the opponent’s forearm as the fish—hence 釣抜 (Tsurinuki, ‘fishing-draw’).”
At this point, use a whole-room cue:
“Everyone—using imagery like this helps you remember names and motions, so please adopt it.”
When you insert beginner-oriented explanations, long-time students can lose a sense of involvement because they already “know” the material. Borrow a page from Akashiya Sanma (To be described later): actively throw the conversation to others. Ask questions, speak to specific people, make eye contact—interactive pull-ins spark ownership. Also remember: even “known” items are not always mastered or remembered. If something is off, correct it; if something is unknown, have them recognize it and learn it. Teach the group that “beginner explanations” are not to be tuned out.
“Throwing the conversation” is a vital technique beyond teaching. People poor at communication rarely do it; they monologue or only speak to the easy partner. Practice it deliberately—even if it feels contrived.
If your explanation is running long, give explicit parallel instructions:
“If you already get it, go ahead and start. (name)-san, follow along with me as I explain.”
When being considerate of others, don’t just say “work individually”; add concrete orders suited to the moment:
“First, confirm the form—shu 守, deliberately.” / “It’s cold today, so warm up by doing shu 守 and ‘kyū 急’ (fast).”
If the group is passive, specify counts and time:
“Do ten draws each.” / “Let’s focus hard for five minutes.”
________________
(ii) Ask another instructor / leader / senior to give side-by-side help
This is easier than (i). The pitfall: when someone is assigned to help one person, progress can stall. If the newcomer needs foundational pieces, time disappears. So give clear instructions to the helper, e.g.:
“That part matters, so we’ll move the group to the next technique while (name)-san keeps working Tsurube-nuki. Please avoid over-teaching details—I won’t have anything left to teach!”
A little humor helps. If it’s getting too granular:
“It’s chilly—let’s keep things brisk and move on.” / “You’re over-explaining! If you dump that much in at once, their brain will explode!”
Don’t just “leave it with them.” Use happōmoku 八方目—keep circling back with quick checks.
Choosing which senior to ask is delicate. Teaching is valuable training in itself, but forcing it on someone can backfire.
________________
(iii) Have them copy a rough outline by sight for now
Also an easier option—but remember you’re still asking for something hard. Help them succeed: go slower; place an instructor or leader next to them a little forward so they can see; position yourself where your angle is clearest.
The first call-out matters most. If you line them up and leave them without stating exactly what you want, that’s instructional abandonment. Make it clear you know they can’t do it yet and invite them to enjoy the tall order.
Check in from time to time. Don’t nitpick; correct only critical errors (front-back reversed stance, left-right directions flipped, etc.). Critical errors are, in fact, the easiest to fix. Fine details won’t land yet and will only halt the whole class. For better or worse, an instructor must read the faces of everyone in the room.
________________
These are examples; choose according to the situation. Above all, avoid sloppy, groundless choices like “eh, this should be fine.” Stopgaps usually aren’t stopping any gaps.
✓ The key is to choose with a clear objective.
2) Keep the Balance
Use the approaches above flexibly, and keep a close eye on the room’s overall mood. Never forget the balance between beginners/first-timers and the rest.
When you do take extra time for beginners—because it matters—don’t just teach silently. Say, for example: “This is crucial for newcomers, so we’ll spend a moment on it. But everyone else—of course you can do this—please understand it well enough to teach it. It’s not someone else’s problem!”
In practice, what you present “for beginners” often exposes gaps: even long-time students may not actually be doing it, or may never have internalized certain facts. Prevent the “we’ve heard this before…” slump by making clear it concerns everyone.
An instructor must read every face, sense the atmosphere, and aim to maximize practice satisfaction for the greatest number.
You’re setting the cadence, explaining, and demonstrating—often all at once. In truth, a good instructor is a kind of superhuman; it’s not ordinary work. Yet precisely this act of attentive control is the essence of hyoho (strategy). It’s where we see whether what you’ve learned is mere stick-swinging or a living practical art.
As you know, Tenshinryu is complex and information-dense, and books that synthesize it are still insufficient. To make explanations easier, we are creating an Introductory Compendium, technique manuals, and teaching guides. These will break items down into minimum prerequisites, basic step-by-step instruction, and cautions—so you can “grab and go” only what’s needed to explain clearly.
With that in hand, you can allocate more mental bandwidth to everything besides explaining. The point is to make easy what can be made easy. Our aim ahead is a manual that guarantees a baseline standard of instruction.
✓ Treat everyone as a favorite—practice “equal favoritism” toward all.
3) Don’t Leave Anyone Behind
What matters is small acts of consideration. A single word of acknowledgment boosts likeability and energizes practice. For example: “What we just did was 点抜 (ten-nuki), a basic drawing method without footwork. From here we’ll move to 動抜 (dō-nuki), which uses footwork—so the difficulty goes up.”
Detailed explanation is important, but offering a light map of where we’re going both saves time and steadily builds Tenshinryu literacy.
People don’t only remember what they study hard; they remember what they hear often. Repetition like this gives even non-beginners a chance to cement knowledge. As noted, with books and manuals still incomplete, repeatedly inputting key terms is essential if you want students to grasp and retain them.
If a beginner looks overwhelmed by too much information, lighten the mood and reassure them: “We’ll do the fine points one-on-one later,” or “It’s fine to keep it rough for now—if you nail it perfectly already, I’ll have nothing left to teach!” Such follow-ups ease anxiety and make the space more comfortable.
✓ In the early stage, everyone craves attention.
________________
4) Throw Them the Topic
The very beginning—before relationships are formed—is your best window for building communication. With a playful “You’ll never get this much attention again—only right now!”, toss them prompts and give them moments in the spotlight. This naturally makes it easier for them to interact with others.
Even when the room is buzzing with in-jokes, a dash of background and a tossed question can erase isolation and create a sense of belonging.
Think of welcoming a transfer student. At the same time, remember that focusing only on the newcomer can spark jealousy or loneliness in others. Use happōmoku 八方目 “eyes in eight directions”—to keep watch over everyone.
On television, skilled MCs deftly “work” a stage full of guests.Akashiya Sanma, one of Japan’s most famous comedians, has showcased exceptionally refined hosting skills. He deftly brings non-celebrity guests into the conversation, gives them chances to speak, draws out their personalities and key details in a short time, and elevates the entire program. I have studied these methods and applied them extensively in my own instruction.
You might think this requires a born class leader or charismatic star. I wasn’t—far from it. In grade school I was the “uncool” kid; even in high school I wasn’t at the center. Of course I now have martial skill, but the world isn’t easy enough for that alone to make you the hub. This is a trained skill—honed by consciously choosing how to stand, speak, and act, including studying how Sanma “works the room” instead of watching purely for entertainment.
✓ Train your ability to “work the room.”
________________
5) Switch to Individualized Instruction
Sometimes the better move is to teach foundations separately. If progress stalls because a beginner or visitor simply can’t keep up, switch to one-on-one. If there are several, carve out time for a beginner/visitor mini-group.
Practically, this means pulling one person—or a few—into a side track and having yourself, another instructor, a leader, or (when appropriate) a senior student lead fundamental drills.
This is effective when the rhythm of practice has broken down and the atmosphere has grown tense for both the beginner and the room.
There is no royal road in instruction or technique. Adapt your approach to the situation.
✓ Be flexible and respond to the moment.
________________
Individualized Instruction
Here are common pitfalls when teaching one-on-one rather than leading the whole group. It may feel easier than group work, but in reality it isn’t.
________________
1) Failing to state the prerequisites
What a beginner needs first is not a movement trick but an understanding of what the technique is trying to do and in what scenario—in other words, a shared vision. If teacher and student hold even a rough common picture, it’s far easier to set guide rails for movement.
For example:
“Tsurube-nuki cuts straight down on an opponent directly in front at close range, with the tip traveling from  the opponent’s crown through the chin. There are many cutting methods, but because this is an introductory drill for learning a big downswing, you lower the sword until the tip nearly touches the floor.”
We tend to think that demonstrating once lets the learner “get it,” but the brain struggles to process actions whose purpose is unclear; copying steps becomes harder, and memorizing them becomes harder still.
So: state the setup first, so the learner can form a common visual image. Briefly give the technique name and its origin; the label helps it go into the brain as a distinct item and “stick.”
Even when a student’s movement is still shaky, I sometimes have them work with a partner. Without facing an actual counterpart, the image stays vague and drifts away from reality. Partner work sharpens the image and leads back to correct solo practice; once the picture is clearer, we return to solo reps.
✓ Always start with the overview, not with steps and cautions.
________________
2) Overlong explanations
It’s tempting to unload everything you know, but what matters is getting the body moving. If you start from fine details, your explanation will swallow most of the training time.
This isn’t just a beginner issue—no one can absorb unlimited information at once. Give the outline and the basic sequence, then make repetition the default.
If the person is self-starting (and not veering into “my own way”), you can ignore non-critical issues and only flag fatal errors. Or touch only the key point:
“Right now it’s doing X; try Y instead.”
Some people won’t move unless you do it with them while they watch. And early on, even tiny steps are hard to remember. If you see confusion or loss of confidence, say “Let’s do it together,” and demonstrate—that little kindness matters.
✓ Don’t try to say everything.
________________
3) Don’t omit or abridge
Beginners are like old computers: they hit capacity and freeze. A classic trap is nōtō (sheathing). When the technique itself is still vague, you start teaching nōtō in detail—and the clock vanishes. If today’s goal is the technique, omit the sheathing sequence for now. Keep asking: what should I be teaching this person right now?
Overwatering a potted plant can cause root rot. Likewise, too much fertilizer can lead to what’s called “fertilizer burn,” which can also kill the plant.
Short-term memory decays quickly. Trim where you can and pack in short, repeated reps to promote retention. The secret to lasting progress is converting items efficiently to long-term memory. If you fail to load the brain and body sufficiently, the gains will fade before the next session.
✓ Reduce the number of tasks.
________________
4) Ignoring priorities, failing to focus
For beginners especially, memorizing the sequence matters most. The same applies to experienced people who haven’t truly fixed the sequence in mind and body: precise tweaks won’t take. Many teachers and students share the same misconception: “If we prevent any wrong shapes by correcting every detail toward a perfect form, improvement will follow.” Except for outright wrong steps or movements, fine polish requires that the pattern be fixed once—even crudely. Try carving a still-gelatinous form and you only make it wobblier. First let it set; then shave or add clay.
Judge where the person is. If the sequence itself is fuzzy, don’t pour on micro-corrections.
Set a clear practice target for this person right now. Share it if you can; even if you don’t, set it for yourself each time. Teaching without a target is like giving a plant the wrong fertilizer, overexposing it to sun, and forgetting to water it. Clear targets yield clear priorities and keep you in focus.
✓ For now, have them memorize the sequence and movements—even roughly.
________________
5) Demanding a perfect score
One of Tenshin Sensei’s weak points as a teacher. Long ago he snapped at a first-day student—“That’s why you’re hopeless!”—and I called him out for it. Of course, failing to attempt what one can do is mere excuse-making. But scolding someone for not meeting a level they’re not ready for only stunts growth.
“A journey of a thousand miles begins with a single step” is a very well-known proverb in Japan; it originates with Laozi.
In the original saying: “A great tree grows from a shoot as fine as a hair; a nine-story tower rises from a heap of earth; a journey of a thousand li begins beneath one’s feet.”
Growth comes from steady accumulation, one step at a time. If you rush, you only risk harming your body and draining your motivation.
Set a passing mark appropriate to each person’s ability and stage. If someone lacks sports background and seems shaky, set it lower and praise what they do achieve. If they have prior budō or clear aptitude, set it a bit higher. Push too hard and they’ll panic over what they can’t do; make it too easy and there’s no challenge—praise may even feel patronizing.
「人を見て法を説け」— tailor the teaching to the person (the Buddha’s taiki seppō).
People differ. Don’t demand one level from all; set today’s passing mark for each. Without this adjustment, piling on “that’s wrong” only breeds confusion and even regression.
✓ Set a passing mark for the moment.
________________
6) Hovering too much
Depending on overall circumstances—especially when staff is thin and students are many—you must balance your attention. Beginners and visitors should get special handling; they are like travelers lost with an unfamiliar map. Don’t treat them as locals. As guides, we should convey how wonderful this “town” is.
But in Tenshinryu, every student is special. If you stare only at the travelers, the locals will start giving you cold looks. Periodically scan the whole floor; if general instruction is lacking, say “Please keep repeating what we just did,” and go help the wider group.
Handled this way, the specially attended person also realizes they’re receiving extra care and appreciates it.
Over-protecting beginners can even lead to something like separation anxiety (seen in pets): they can’t function unless the “owner” (teacher) is right there. Build lateral ties to prevent this: instead of doing everything yourself, ask another student, “Could you keep an eye on them for a bit?”
Always keep track of the whole room. It’s excellent to teach with all your might, but if you become so absorbed in one person that the other students—or the group as a whole—are left unattended, that’s a problem.
✓ Keep a little distance—help, but don’t hover.
________________
7) Failing to warn about risks
With beginners and visitors, injury and accidents are the biggest fears. Even without injury, breaking newly purchased gear—or loaner weapons—is painful. Common mishaps include bracing a hand on the floor in a fast move or cracking a scabbard. Overexertion can strain joints or cause muscle tears. Tell them not to overdo it.
Tenshin Sensei sometimes worries over-protectively. His timing can be off and the warning ineffective—but we should emulate the consideration itself.
✓ A light scare is about right.
________________
The Secret Is to Make It Enjoyable
It is a problem if martial training becomes only fun. Without seriousness, injuries follow. Balance is essential—but especially for beginners and trial students, instructors must use every means available to make them want to keep training.
The era when “only the chosen survive, only the chosen improve” is long over; a tradition cannot endure on that mindset. That truth was evident as far back as the Shōwa period, yet many schools failed to grasp it. Trainees dwindled in number, their level declined, the art’s appeal suffered, and new enrollment fell further—a negative spiral. What remained was not a “select elite,” but merely a small, non-select few—a sorry state one could call a small and weak minority.
Many martial artists have thundered, “True martial arts are nothing but blood, sweat, and tears—pure hardship! Anything else is soft, a sham, and anyone who can’t endure it has no right to train. We don’t need weak students!” In practice, however, that creed drove numbers down, left the community aging, eroded technical standards, and set entire traditions on a steady path of decline and enfeeblement. It is a deeply melancholy reality.
Tenshinryu must break out of that spiral. Unless every instructor fosters a training hall that is serious and bright, enjoyable, and fruitful, we will not achieve what we could.
Like it or not, in the modern world teaching is a form of service work. Especially since this is culture, not a daily necessity or a direct vocation for most, we instructors cannot avoid being service professionals.
Yet a person who overflows with that service spirit—attentive in every sense—will be liked and trusted anywhere, even after laying down the sword, taking off the gi, and leaving the dōjō. That, in daily life, is precisely Lord Munenori’s katsujinken—the “life-giving sword”—made real.
There is a story from another school: an elderly instructor treated students so poorly that the headmaster admonished him—“You’re lucky to be called ‘sensei.’ At our age, out in the world, young people won’t listen to us or give us the time of day. But in the dōjō they call you ‘Sensei’ and hear you out. Be grateful.”
How lonely to step out of the dōjō and be “just another person.” Cast off the notion that you are doing students a favor by teaching; be grateful to be allowed to teach, and work each day to optimize your instruction. Too many martial instructors, flattered by constant cries of “Sensei, Sensei,” forget their beginnings, lean back, and merely posture. Some even spend their time on social media criticizing others, spreading a poor image of the arts.
By taking such negative examples as cautionary lessons and continually refining your conduct, words and deeds, and teaching, you will, in time, transcend your innate endowments and become a true master in the fullest sense—mind, skill, and body in harmony.

Afterword
How did this read?
In this volume, I analyzed and classified the notoriously hard-to-explain topic of teaching beginners and first-time visitors—and the ripple effects this has on group instruction. Both explaining and understanding these matters can be difficult, but I hope this book helps you see them with a bit more structure and clarity.
I am still very much a work in progress. After every practice I hold a “post-mortem” in my head. Settling for “good enough” is not the way; aiming—even slightly—for better instruction each time is, I believe, a defining condition of a good teacher. That mindset is the true root of humility and growth.
If you have questions—“What should I do in this situation?”—please ask. I will add supplements and revisions accordingly. Thank you for reading. All is for the betterment of the tradition.
Tenshinryu Hyoho — 10th Shike,
Kuwami Masakumo
