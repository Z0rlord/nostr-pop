# Conditions for Instructors

﻿Conditions for Instructors
Kuwami Masakumo
________________
Below are the conditions required of instructors in Tenshinryu Hyoho. Each is essential. After the bullet list, detailed explanations of each point are provided. I hope you will become an exemplary instructor whose achievements will be honored by future generations.
* As an instructor, devote yourself to teaching with passion and fulfill your responsibilities.
* Above all, commit to your own improvement and lead by example in the conduct and training attitude of a good student.
* As a representative of Tenshinryu, be conscious of your role, uphold the honor of the tradition, cultivate personal virtue, and become a person recognized by society.
* Work diligently to promote Tenshinryu.
* Dedicate yourself wholeheartedly to the cultivation of students.
* Refrain from excessive self-sacrifice and act in the spirit of coexistence and mutual prosperity.
As an instructor, devote yourself to teaching with passion and fulfill your responsibilities
In Tenshinryu, we place the highest value on self-motivation and initiative. In this day and age, it would be nothing short of nonsense to try to teach a traditional martial art by relying on coercion.
Passion is the greatest weapon and the key to progress. Yet an instructor also bears responsibility. Precisely because of that responsibility, exceptional improvement can be expected. A sense of responsibility, too, arises from one’s own initiative and cannot be forced.
What an instructor needs most are passion and a sense of responsibility.
Certainly, there are many other qualities desirable in an instructor: excellent technique, clear explanations and speaking skills, strong leadership, organizational management, and so on. Abilities vary from person to person, and an instructor can still function even if one of these is lacking. However, there are also qualities without which one cannot stand as an instructor.
Those are passion (drive, motivation, energy) and a sense of responsibility.
As stated above, Tenshinryu values self-motivation. Therefore, no matter how proficient someone becomes, we do not entrust them with an instructor’s role unless they themselves seek it.
To aspire to be an instructor is to require an indispensable sense of mission and responsibility, and to overcome every difficulty that accompanies those duties.
Moreover, passion, motivation, and a sense of responsibility are all capacities that can be acquired after the fact. Of course there are individual differences, and some people possess these as natural aptitudes, but most outstanding people—whether in teaching or in other fields—did not rely on innate talent or wait for fortune to bestow such qualities upon them. They work to rouse and sustain their passion and motivation, and they strive to cultivate a deep-rooted sense of responsibility.
Above all, strive for your own improvement and lead by example in what it means to be a good student and in training attitude
「人一能之己百之。人十能之己千之。」
Countless people lament their lack of talent, yet almost no one acknowledges, and then actually acts on, the need to work twice as hard precisely because they lack talent. This line comes from the Chinese classic Zhongyong (The Doctrine of the Mean) and carries the following meaning: if a gifted person can accomplish something with one attempt, I will do it a hundred times; if a gifted person requires ten attempts, I will do it a thousand.
An instructor must be the most dedicated practitioner in the dojo—the one who can work the hardest.
To speak frankly, the prosperity of a dojo does not necessarily rise in direct proportion to an instructor’s personal advancement. At times, it can even become an obstacle. This is because, for learners, it is psychologically easier when the teacher’s level appears fixed as a clear goal. If the teacher keeps improving, it can feel as though the goal keeps receding.
That said, mastering Tenshinryu cannot be achieved through ordinary effort. The martial skills that samurai once drove themselves to master—at the risk of their lives in the early Edo period—cannot possibly be easy for modern people. To pass this tradition on to the next generation at the highest standard, without letting it become an empty shell, it is essential that all students train with a view to continual improvement. We believe this attitude must be the same for instructors and, indeed, even for the Shike (head of the school).
Be conscious that you represent Tenshinryu, uphold the tradition’s honor, cultivate personal virtue, and become someone recognized by society
All students should be aware that once you call yourself a student of Tenshinryu, society will regard each of you as a representative of Tenshinryu.
All the more so for instructors: every word you speak and every manner of conduct will be taken as that of Tenshinryu itself.
‘Traditional martial arts’ may sound admirable, but the fact remains that we inherit techniques that contain violence and can be dangerous if misused.
To study and transmit weapons skills that are unnecessary in the modern age requires that we maintain a healthy relationship with society. Those who pursue this path must possess excellent character, exert a positive influence on students and others, and be recognized as socially useful — both as individuals and as members of Tenshinryu.
It is true that preserving samurai culture and the use of ancient weapons is anachronistic. But if we posture as outlaws or display antisocial attitudes, we only diminish the social value of preserving traditional martial arts and end up undermining ourselves.
Dedicate yourself wholeheartedly to the cultivation of students
What is truly alarming is being called ‘sensei,’ letting it go to your head, and losing the drive to keep striving. Instructors who fall into this trap often fear their students’ growth and try to hold it back. Believing they themselves have no room left to improve, they begin to worry that their position will be threatened as their students advance.
Teaching, by its nature, is one of the most effective means of accelerating one’s own progress. Instructors are entrusted with responsibility, and fulfilling that responsibility requires deeper mastery of technique. By moving from the position of being taught to the position of teaching, you confront the techniques head-on and come to understand them more profoundly.
Work diligently to promote Tenshinryu
Instructors are, so to speak, the vanguard for spreading Tenshinryu. We must present Tenshinryu to as many people as possible as an open path, and warmly welcome new entrants as juniors who will walk that same path.
Traditional martial arts tend to close their doors and be exclusive. In former times that might have passed as a samurai custom, but today we live in an egalitarian society where everyone has the right to choose.
The mindset of ‘It is enough if only those who understand will understand’ or ‘Only those who truly seek it should come’ is precisely a high-handed, lordly way of doing things. In a modern world overflowing with entertainment options, such an attitude will be cast aside without even making it onto the table for consideration.
We also believe in human potential. Each of us always carries boundless possibility, and anyone can change. Of course, there are practical limits. My height will not suddenly become 185 cm, and no matter how strongly you believe in potential, you cannot just step out and do a spacewalk. But unless you first believe in possibility, even what could be done will end up not being done.
Giving up is the path that leads farthest away from success.
Refrain from excessive self-sacrifice and act in the spirit of coexistence and mutual prosperity
A certain measure of devotion and self-sacrifice is indispensable for instructors. However, in Japanese martial arts, the self-sacrifice of instructors has long been treated as the only means of keeping traditions alive.
The result has been an endless demand for endurance from everyone involved — a kind of so-called passion exploitation — in which people have prolonged a tradition by sacrificing their time, finances, and labor.
Naturally, such an unhealthy state cannot last. Many traditions have already been lost, and many more remain in danger of being lost even now.
We must put an end to this negative cycle, which bears the name of a strategy for survival but is anything but.
For that reason, even if we face criticism, we must ensure a reasonable margin of benefit. Unless we create a situation in which we prosper, others prosper, and everyone involved can share in genuine well-being, the sustainable preservation of tradition will remain a distant dream.
