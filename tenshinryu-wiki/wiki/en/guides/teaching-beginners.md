# Guidelines for Teaching Beginners

Instructor guide by **Kuwami Masakumo** (鍬海政雲), 10th-generation headmaster (*Tenshinryu Hyoho*). Focus: **beginners and trial participants**.

> Source PDF lists author as “Masakumo Kuwakai” — likely typographical; identity matches 10th Shike Kuwami Masakumo.

## Context

Tenshinryu lacks a single standardized instructor manual; Master Tenshin’s teaching style left instruction methods diverse. Mixed-level classes (many beginners + trial students) easily become chaotic — beginners feel neglected, seniors feel held back.

**Instructor stance:** Improve your own teaching rather than blame students. Teaching is **service**, not command.

This guide covers **group instruction** and **individual instruction**.

---

## Group instruction

Hardest format: circle or rows, mixed skill levels.

### Principle 1 — Give clear instructions; decide an approach

When beginners are present, announcing a technique name alone is insufficient. **Never leave beginners unattended** (instructional neglect).

Three approaches:

#### (1) Dedicated explanation for beginners

Explain purpose and motion *before* the group drills.

Example (*Suketa* 助け太刀 / scooping draw):

> “Step diagonally forward while drawing as if scooping upward from below…”

Goal is **not** perfect performance — give a reference so they can attempt with confidence. Fear of the unknown stops people when told only “watch and copy.”

- Weave in terminology naturally (e.g. **koiguchi** 鯉口 when teaching *Sōsen*).
- Keep advanced students engaged: questions, eye contact, storytelling.

#### (2) Assistants for individual guidance

Easier for head instructor; risk of slowing the class.

Direct assistants clearly:

> “Important detail — we’re moving on. Have [name] practice that aside; don’t overload them.”

Check assistant progress so class flow holds.

#### (3) Mimic general movement

Adapt for visibility: slower demo, better angles, clearer positioning.

Set expectations explicitly:

> “Just copy the general motion — doesn’t have to be perfect. Give it a go!”

Periodically check beginners without breaking overall rhythm.

### Principle 2 — Balance beginner and senior training

When spending extra time on foundations, tell seniors **why**:

> “This is essential foundation — even experienced students should teach at this level of detail. Pay attention!”

Prevents seniors from mentally checking out.

---

## Individual instruction

Seems easier; common pitfalls:

### 1. Missing context

Explain **situation and purpose** before movement.

Example (*Tsurube-nuki*):

> Downward cut to head/chin; fundamental for large cutting — swing low, near the floor.

Without context, purpose stays opaque.

### 2. Over-explaining

Teach **general movement first**; refine details over time.

If hesitant: “Let’s do this together — follow me.”

### 3. Expecting perfection too soon

Set **appropriate passing bars** per student:

- Lower bar + praise for coordination struggles
- Higher bar for prior experience

---

## Make training enjoyable

Discipline matters, but beginners quit if never engaged. Tenshinryu instructors should be **serious and approachable** — welcoming, enjoyable, productive.

Good instructors earn respect in and outside the dojo. Refining teaching refines the self.
