# Miden Kurai-no-Koto

Textbook on **Kurai (位)** — Tenshinryu body postures for combat — by **10th Shike Kuwami Masakumo**. In the school this general teaching is called **Miden (身伝)**: transmission of the fundamental fighting body.

> Kurai appear throughout [[techniques/tachiai-12-kata]] (Kumoi, Sekiun, Seigan, Ogesa, Hasso, etc.). This book explains what they are and why there are so many.

## Why this book exists

Modern students (Japanese and foreign) lack Edo-period lifestyle context; practice time is limited; information volume is huge. Online lessons, video, and kinetic anatomy supplement instruction — **this text supports study of basic and demonstration kurai** seen in enbu and social media.

## Kurai vs Kamae

| Term | Tenshinryu usage |
|------|------------------|
| **Kurai (位)** | Official term — state/position; tactical control of enemy formation (陣形) |
| **Kamae (構え)** | Common budo word; avoided as fixed/subjective, though *kamaeru* may appear in practice |

Kurai must stay **relative** — optimized to enemy posture, terrain, weather, and your own condition. Fixed forms exist **for learning principles**, not for rigid combat.

## Why so many kurai?

**Ishii Seizo (8th Shike):** *"There are four seasons in Tenshinryu's Kurai."*

Dojo floors are flat and sheltered; real ground is uneven, muddy, wooded, cramped (low ceilings, hallways). *"Dojo training is blocked by walls. There is no end to the wilderness."*

Many kurai → train mind–technique–body (**心技体**) from multiple angles; reduce surprise at unknown enemy stances. Sun Tzu: know enemy and self (*知彼知己者百戦不殆*).
