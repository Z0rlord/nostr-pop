# On Reiho (Etiquette)

*2018-10-03* — Traditional culture

Related wiki pages: [[reiho/_index|Reiho index]] · [[reiho/technique-of-respect|The Technique of Respect]]

## What is reiho?

> “Man is a social animal.”

This is Aristotle, student of Plato. Humans are independent living beings, yet we form society as groups. We are self-centered beings who must also cultivate sociality to coexist with others.

If everyone moved only for immediate self-interest without regard for others, society would fill with suspicion and sound human relations would be impossible.

At the same time, as the saying goes — “a small kindness, a big nuisance” — one-sided “consideration” that ignores the other’s position and feelings also damages relationships.

“**Reiho requires heart above all; one must not cling only to form.**”

Read any modern etiquette book and you will find this line almost without exception.

Today there is a tendency to speak of etiquette and manners as **spirit alone**, as if form had been lost.

Certainly consideration and thoughtfulness should arise from the subtle movements of the human heart.

But form known without freedom leads to chaos — not breaking form but having **no** form becomes common.

As a standard that creates right and wrong, I feel that in this chaotic modern age we are asked to look again at **reiho** and **sahō** (作法) as “form” that Japan has cultivated over long ages.

Daily we risk losing our inner axis and spiritual pillar amid diversifying values.

**Reiho** is a common code of conduct born within a particular society — reason cultivated by civilization. It is not mere manual: form aligns and heart appears; heart aligns and appears in form.

```
From nothing, form arises.
Form seeks heart.
Heart again seeks form.
Within heart there is form.
Within form there is heart.
All returns to nothing.
```
— Tenshinryu Hyoho *dōka* (道歌)

## On simultaneous movement (*nagara dōsa*)

In martial technique we generally move **as continuously as possible** so the enemy cannot read our action. Techniques are mostly performed **in parallel** — not step by step.

“**While placing the hand on the sword, drop to kneeling seiza, open the saya while rising on one knee, draw and cut**” — countless actions contained in one motion make the technique work.

If you segment: “Place hand on sword. Sit in seiza. Stand on both knees. Open saya. Step forward into iai posture. Draw. Cut” — it is easier to learn and perform, but chopped into pieces it becomes a technique full of gaps and wind-up, useless in earnest.

Intentionally dividing movement has applications such as feints, but that is application. The enemy before you will not wait for your pace; martial arts dislike rhythmic, predictable motion.

Tenshinryu values doing it in **one beat** (*ippaku*) or **sword, qi, and body as one mass** (*kenki tai ichigan*) — names may differ in other schools or kendo (“one rhythm,” “unity of spirit, sword, and body”), but the theory is largely the same.

## Reiho that dislikes simultaneous movement

For **reiho**, the opposite holds.

Warriors carried the sword always — habit and custom from the mindset of **constant battlefield**. In peacetime, showing alertness, ill will, or hostility toward another would be gravely rude.

Thus every detail matters: where and how the sword is placed, how one moves, each gesture. That attentive mindset is the foundation of **kikubari** and **kokubari** — care and consideration — in reiho.

Even with utmost heart, if rules changed case by case, reception would differ by person; one might still give offense. **Buke reiho** (warrior etiquette) embodied considerate spirit in shared rules.

For armed warriors, **nagara dōsa** — acting in one beat, hard to detect — is unpleasant and alarming. A sword at the hip behind or to the side, or a *kodachi* at the waist, might be drawn instantly.

As in “do not adjust your cap under a plum tree” (*rika ni kan wo shōsezu*), reiho **forbids** nagara actions that provoke vigilance.

In tea ceremony and dining etiquette, returning both hands between moves seems tedious, but such care lies at the root.

Movements that look irrational often contain this intent.

Where martial technique at its peak can seem beautiful as dance, etiquette born from consideration is likewise an aesthetic Japan produced.

## From “reiho that lives in the modern age” to “reiho that serves the modern age”

Using these forms unchanged today can feel utterly unreasonable — useless baggage.

Yet traditional culture of this kind produced Japanese aesthetic sense: finding beauty in quiet movement.

**Shitsuke** (躾) is written with “beautiful body,” but it is not appearance alone — beauty of bearing and speech.

Japanese reiho is tied directly to beauty.

Modern Japanese manners rest on **Ogasawara reiho** plus Western manners after Meiji. That too matters.

Look further back and there is the warrior aesthetic that bushi society developed from court etiquette.

We may use it little unchanged in modern society, but reflecting on aesthetics Japanese cultivated and feeding that back into modern etiquette could restore core and foundation.

Tenshinryu transmits a comparatively large body of **buke reiho**.

Martial arts do not exist to learn etiquette — yet in an age with few chances to learn reiho, that side too, though not the main purpose, plays an important role in inheriting Japanese culture.

We disciples, myself included, are not praised for refinement in reiho, but daily we study the school’s transmitted etiquette and unravel buke reiho in self-cultivation.

We hope eventually **reiho that lives in the modern age** may rise to **reiho that serves the modern age**.
