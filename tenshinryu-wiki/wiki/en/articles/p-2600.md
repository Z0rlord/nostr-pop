## “One mesh cannot catch a bird; the bird-catching mesh is that one mesh”

In **Hirosaki-han bugei bunsho wo yomu**, the Arai family transmission **Hōzōin jūmonji yari kyojō vol. 2** records:

- *Hitome-ra fukatoku tori* — one mesh cannot catch a bird  
- *Tokutori ra wa kore hitome nari* — the mesh that catches the bird is one mesh  

*Ra* (羅) = net. Throwing nets and fixed **hariami** (張り網): one eye alone cannot catch a bird — yet the bird is caught in **one** eye. Too large a net is hard to throw; too large a fixed net is hard to set and retrieve — limits are obvious — yet the phrase expresses technique’s nature well.

**Kasumiami** mist nets are now restricted hunting tools in Japan.

The same lines appear in Hirosaki **Hongaku Kokki-ryū** preface (1651). A fine metaphor — but not originally martial.

### Older sources (optional deep dive)

The phrase appears before 1651 in:

- **Chūkōsen** (注好選, before 1152) — tales including “birds caught in one eye of the net” (vol. 3)
- **Ōjōyōshū** (985) — “One mesh cannot catch a bird; myriad methods assist contemplation toward the great matter of birth in the Pure Land” — Buddhist practice needs many “meshes”
- **Tōdaiji fujubun** (9th c.) — many teachings, one truth; many saints, one saves a person
- **Saichō** (806) — one mesh cannot catch a bird; one sect alone cannot save all
- **Makahshikan** (594, China) — cast wide dharma nets to catch the bird of mind — **taiji hōben**, teaching suited to the listener
- **Huainanzi** (*Enanji*, 2nd c. BCE) — “When birds come, spread the net; what catches is one eye. If only one eye, you never catch birds.” Also: net without bait catches no fish; rudeness to a gentleman wins no wisdom; dodder grows without roots; snake moves without feet — things have their way.

Japanese koryū transmission documents show **strong Buddhist influence**; systems like **menkyo kaiden** may originate there.

## Main topic

Ishii Sensei said: “**In real combat, only two or three techniques are usable.**”

Not identical to nets, but broadly true — the opening trigger is usually **one** move. Many koryū aim at fingers and hands first, then **todome**.

Manga berserk instant bisection — except surprise — is not realistic in a real exchange.

Collecting kata without usable skill wastes time; time spent learning many lowers depth in each — obvious.

Until a few years ago Nakamura Sensei was pre-retirement, raising children alone — little regular keiko time. Battojutsu centered; kenjutsu barely reached. Even batto had limited time for many techniques; kenjutsu **tōhō** (刀法) were few. Nakamura Sensei said: “**Many techniques exist, but you cannot use them all in real fight.**”

After retirement, with stable keiko time, many more techniques are taught. Whether all are used in combat is another question — **having many techniques has proper reasons** — one is reducing the danger of **not knowing** (*masaka*, from Part I).

Another is **individuality**. Perfect mastery of all is hard even with time; among many forms each person finds **tokui waza** — techniques and movement that suit them. Same curriculum, different ease and strength — in combat, two or three “mesh eyes” become your center; you spread the net around them.

Tenshinryu offers a **large net** with countless eyes so each person’s character can emerge.
