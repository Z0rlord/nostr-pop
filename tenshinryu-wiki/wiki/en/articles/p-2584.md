## Learning bujutsu is learning kata

To study bujutsu / budō is basically to study **kata**. The character **型** is standard. “Form of culture” exists — culture itself can be seen as inherited tangible and intangible **form**. Japan is called a “kata culture,” yet having **form** is not unique to Japan.

Boxers learn combinations — a kind of kata. Basketball, volleyball, tennis — drilling situational patterns in practice is ordinary, not remarkable. What marks Japanese culture is tending to center on kata and dislike large changes to those forms.

For practical use, when problems appear in application, changing the form naturally follows — yet bujutsu, though adapted to times, typically preserves fundamental kata strongly: a classic of kata culture.

Today, koryū with the Japanese sword as measure can no longer fulfill original battlefield utility; preserving kata becomes important under a new primary aim: **preservation of tradition**.

## Japan and the sword

The idea that kata is a great feature of Japanese culture has long been debated. That bushi culture and traditional bujutsu survived to the present may be the strongest evidence.

Western sword arts largely vanished early with firearms; fencing preserves traces, it is said. Fechtbücher show European knightly sword arts were advanced with set forms, but culture did not continue — reconstruction works from manuscripts today.

Japanese attachment to traditional bujutsu may be attachment to the Japanese sword — visible even in WWII military swords. Carrying a longsword to a modern battlefield would have drawn stares in the West (though it happened — see **Mad Jack** below).

### Mad Jack

British Army Captain **Jack Churchill** fought in WWII with longsword and even bow — nickname Mad Jack. “An officer should not go to battle without his sword” — old knightly creed. In 1941 he killed an NCO with a longbow — “the only recorded British soldier in WWII to kill with a bow.” Photos exist of him landing with longsword. Bagpipes, commando raids, captured by grenade, escaped twice — tough. But as “Mad Jack” suggests, others saw him as bizarre. Dancing between life and death shows courage not everyone has.

### “Wonderland Japan”

Scholar **Watanabe Shōichi** noted the **Yōrō Code** (757) legally lived until the Meiji Constitution (1889) — over 1100 years, likely dead letter long before abolition. Unusual for one state to keep dead law so long — “Japanese culture keeps things unless they truly get in the way.” He also noted abroad: “To study classical socialism, go to Japan” — while socialist states updated theory, Japan alone seemed to preserve classical socialism on the shelf.

Firearms spread yet Japanese clung to the sword until the end of shogunate, then again to WWII — whether to call this “fixation on form” is for cultural scholars; it is uniquely interesting.

## From *kata* to *seihō*

Fixation on **kata** has merit and fault. When practical need demands change, avoiding even gentle revision until a sudden catastrophic break — **Meiji Restoration** may exemplify.

Cherishing kata strongly aids inheriting traditional culture — yet fixation can freeze movement.

Tenshinryu **taboos** kata as rigid mold unable to adapt. Hence we write **勢法** (*seihō*) and read it **kata**.

**勢** — posture of movement; **法** — principle, method, concept. Read deeply: **optimal action in a given situation**. Seihō is not mere mold; it exists to learn bodily, technical, tactical, strategic theorems and constants **including** freedom from fixation — therefore **勢法 (kata)**.

## Dismissal of kata

Japan is kata culture yet also changes all at once, uniformly, when it changes.

From mid-Edo, shinai and armor made safe competition easier; school barriers fell and **kata** was belittled. Stable Tokugawa rule drastically reduced real combat; kata hollowed, became absurd or pursued beauty — **kage kenjutsu** (华法剣法, “flowery sword”) mocked shinai schools.

Light shinai contact favored shinai mechanics over curved, heavy, shorter katana — kata for real blades seemed useless in shinai “battle” — inevitable.

That view reaches modern kendo. Shinai spread to many koryū; shogunate encouraged it; domain schools often mandated shinai **gekken** — only a few resisted (Tenshinryu in our line did not adopt shinai keiko).

Jujutsu too saw randori widely in late Edo; kendo and judo after Meiji reorganized budō around competition — kata dismissal grew.

From Edo to Heisei, most practitioners questioned kata’s meaning: “Will this work?” “Does real fight go this way?” “Randori / shiai makes you stronger?” “Isn’t it just fixed patterns like folk performance?”

Opponents never move as promised. Standard attack-defense forms help, but overly diverse kata — divorced from combat — seem pointless.

In peaceful Edo, kata already diverged, grew complex or ornamental — **kage kenjutsu** criticism — yet criticism is older still.

## Musashi’s point

In **Gorin no Sho**, *Kaze no Maki*:

**On other schools with many sword techniques** — boasting many techniques to sell the Way as deep to beginners — Musashi calls this **hateful in martial arts**: many options breed hesitation in combat. Doraemon’s pocket scene — “not this, not that” — no time to choose when life hangs on an instant.

Yet **by place and circumstance**, five directions (*goho*) — five forms — should exist; Gorin no Sho records five; extras were later additions by disciples. Twisting body and hands, flying and opening — not real cutting; straight body and mind, distort the enemy’s body and **break the twist in the enemy’s heart** — that is Niten Ichiryū’s heart.

Tenshinryu flies, leaps, twists — reads like Musashi bashing us — yet simplicity-as-best appears in **Mushinshinkage-ryū**.

## Mushinshinkage-ryū

Founder **Harigaya Sekiun** (d. 1669), studied Shinkage, then lost use of left arm in a fall (Kōno Yoshinori writes in detail). He opened **Mushinshinkage** — uniquely **only raising the sword to the brow and cutting down**. Absurd — yet nothing else was recognized.

Famous **ainuke** — two masters of Mushinshinkage cannot decide victory; neither moves; transmission completes. 2nd head **Odagiri Ichiun** (1662 license) dueled Sekiun — aïnuke. But 3rd **Mariya Enshirō** struck Ichiun — aïnuke failed after one generation. Still, the ideal was preached: “When sword principle peaks, no cut, quiet bow, withdraw — peace of bu — **heihō**.” Mutual deterrence. Lineage later died out.

Many techniques — “easy to say, hard to do” — yet simply reducing is not the answer either (Niten, Mushinshinkage). Techniques must be judged before adding, or practice wastes time on nonsense. Extreme simplicity limits movement and makes adaptation hard — mastery may end simple, but demanding that of students is hard.

Koryū need not add or subtract for moderns. In Tenshinryu we do not treat overturning, changing, or appending past teaching as right. Application is infinite; transmission is only what the teacher handed down.

Yet clinging only to one’s school traps you — breadth and flexible technique are ideal. In spirit: “**No technique that does not exist in Tenshinryu**” — obviously impossible; a **mindset**.

Example: **Aigane no kurai** — when the enemy’s guard is unusual, copy it like a bell ringing through the body (details not written — learn the meaning yourself; not for full public exposition). Same for techniques: unknown effective methods can trap you; knowing costs nothing.

If nutrition feels lacking, supplement outside school — not forbidden to touch other ryu — but keiko is basically school technique to grow strong through the school.

## The threat of “surely not”

Tenshinryu has many techniques partly because knowing variety reveals countermeasures; ignorance makes response very hard — like playing cards without knowing suits.

Sunzi: know enemy and self — hundred battles no peril; know self not enemy — one win one loss; know neither — every battle peril.

“Enemy” includes unseen future opponents; in kenjutsu, knowing effective means an opponent may use.

Tricks work poorly on the strong in fair duel — but in unrestricted killing, infinite options exist.

Tenshinryu oral teaching: what is terrifying is **“surely not”** (*masaka*). We repeat **fear of the unexpected** through technique — techniques of an age far less open than today; even within a school, not seeing until license was normal (some schools still hide).

Learning countless techniques means knowing existence, effect, counter — but only those who understand and forge themselves to that depth.

Without **awareness**, nothing is gained; hiding true intent is possible — other schools mock “many useless techniques for money and pride” — itself a trick inviting carelessness.

Some schools hide “true form” until rank. Zeami: “Secret makes the flower; without secret, no flower” — ultimate secret: **hide even that you hide**. We are not in layered secrecy now; Tenshinryu shows many once-secret techniques in embu — yet not everything should be published.
