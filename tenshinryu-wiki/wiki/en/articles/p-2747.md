# On Fukuro-Shinai (Bag Bamboo Sword)

*2025-03-26* — About Tenshinryu

In the Shinkage-ryū lineage, a training weapon used in place of a live blade is called **fukuro-shinai** (袋竹刀, “bag bamboo sword”). It is considered the prototype of the modern kendo shinai.

Many schools (including Shinkage-ryū) use wooden swords (*bokutō*) for kenjutsu practice, but as dramas and anime suggest, striking a person with a wooden sword in reality causes fractures — and a blow to the head can be fatal. To cultivate the sensation of contact safely, **Kamiizumi Nobutsuna**, founder of Shinkage-ryū, is said to have devised this fukuro-shinai.

Tenshinryu also uses it especially for kenjutsu practice (occasionally for batto practice as well). Bamboo is covered with cow or horse hide; because the surface resembles toad skin (whether it truly does is uncertain), it is also called **hikihada-shinai** (蟇肌竹刀). In Tenshinryu we call it **marudachi** (丸太刀).

The bamboo of a fukuro-shinai is split into four sections to the midpoint, then eight toward the tip, and in some cases sixteen at the very end. This flex reduces impact and allows safer practice. Overall length is about **three shaku two sun** (~96 cm), the standard sword length. The seam is treated as the edge (some schools reverse this). The surface is lacquered red or black. In Tenshinryu, coating the leather with **kaki-shibu** (persimmon tannin) for durability is the formal method.

*Shinai* can also be read **chikutō**. The reading *shinai* is said to come from the bamboo “bending” (*shinaru*).

Modern kendo shinai are said to have come into use from the mid-Edo period. As noted, fukuro-shinai is called the prototype, but bamboo practice swords were adopted around the same era in other styles, similar to Shinkage-ryū.

When Nakamura Sensei and Ishii Sensei (8th Shike) trained, equipment was scarce (Ishii’s family storehouse had gear, but it was lost when the Ishii storehouse burned in the Pacific War, tradition says). They substituted rolled newspaper or kendo shinai. Nakamura Sensei sometimes hand-made “newspaper swords,” but wrapped so thick and heavy (about twice a fukuro-shinai) that they became dangerous and unusable.

White fukuro-shinai are sold relatively inexpensively today, but Tenshinryu instructs students to lacquer them red or black. Where possible, apply kaki-shibu as well — within what each person can manage, we transmit tradition faithfully. Especially for things the previous generation could not do owing to social or economic circumstances, observing them properly is, I believe, a way of repaying the debt to those who preserved and handed down the school.
