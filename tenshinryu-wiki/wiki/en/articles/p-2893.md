# The Meeting of Nakamura Sensei and Ide Sensei

*2026-02-17* — Essay, history

See also [[people/ide-ryusetsu|Ide Ryusetsu — 11th Shike]] · [[people/nakamura-tenshin|Nakamura Tenshin — 9th Shike]]

The current 11th Shike, **Ide Ryusetsu Sensei**, entered Tenshinryu in university. The trigger was taking seriously a high-school friend’s words (who later also joined): “Ide, we’ve gotta train in kobujutsu as proper Japanese men, right!?”

Both loved *Shura no Mon* and had long admired classical martial arts. Ide agreed enthusiastically and began gathering information.

It was the early 2000s — far less online than today. Searching here and there, the only lead was the **monthly *Hiden* magazine’s special dojo guide**, issued twice a year as a supplement.

(The guide is online now; back then each issue had a dojo corner and summer/winter booklets listed dojo nationwide.)

While comparing many arts in that guide, Ide noticed Tenshinryu — relatively close to home and with a strange presence — and contacted them.

Nakamura Sensei answered the phone warmly: “Please come.” They set a date.

The day before, Ide called to confirm — and **could not get through**.

In fact, Tenshinryu **was not publicly taking disciples** at the time.

Nakamura Sensei had confused Ide with an acquaintance from another school — a teacher whose surname differed by one character — perhaps helped by drink (probably unrelated; Nakamura Sensei often listens poorly and jumps to conclusions). That teacher and Nakamura Sensei had met at embu; “I’ll teach you the sword, let’s meet” — so when Ide called, Nakamura Sensei thought, *finally he’s ready!* and joyfully agreed.

Suddenly told “I’m not taking disciples now,” Ide mentally retorted, *Then don’t list yourself in the dojo guide!* — but Nakamura Sensei said, “**A man doesn’t go back on his word**,” and the next day’s meeting stood.

Ide went alone to Nakamura Sensei’s home — not a traditional house but an ordinary apartment, interior frighteningly cluttered.

At the time Nakamura Sensei was guarded, not eloquent or friendly; his rough manner shocked Ide — “**This is a real martial artist!**”

Meeting one’s first budō teacher as Nakamura Sensei was a rather eccentric experience (true also when I and others met him).

Then, owing to hiding school secrets and predecessors, and backlash from long criticism, he was deeply suspicious — wanting communication yet on maximum alert.

Today the gloomy aura is gone; he seems a very odd but kindly old man. That took long years to melt a heart frozen like permafrost.

After a simple, somewhat unclear explanation of the school and a stunning demonstration of technique, Ide burned to learn — but Nakamura Sensei said he was not taking disciples; look elsewhere.

He was sent to visit another dojo later. For Ide, captivated by Nakamura Sensei and Tenshinryu, joining another school was unthinkable.

Following “look around,” Ide spent about a year using the dojo guide and scant web info, watching embu — yet conviction that **it must be Tenshinryu** never wavered.

Why list in the dojo guide while not taking disciples? Nakamura Sensei had wished to show the school’s name and existence somehow, though he could not take disciples for reasons below.

A year later Ide contacted again: “I searched for a year; for me nothing but Tenshinryu will do.”

**Moss’s single thought pierces stone.** Unable to reject such sustained will, Nakamura Sensei accepted a disciple for the first time in several years.

Before that he had occasionally enrolled children, workplace friends, or whimsical connections — workplace people mainly test-cutting, not much technique to children (there was no proper wide training space anyway).

A few years earlier **Takei Sensei** and one other trained irregularly in insufficient space, striving to preserve the school — repeatedly begging Nakamura Sensei to increase disciples, always sharply refused.

When Ide enrolled, Takei Sensei was greatly surprised — *what happened?*

Initially instruction was at Nakamura Sensei’s family home; after buying the apartment, there. Ide began training in that apartment.

Takei Sensei had trained at the family home; after the move, also at the apartment.

The family home was not sold, but held bitter memories — not a comfortable place for Nakamura Sensei.

Ide trained eagerly soon after enrollment.

About a year later Nakamura Sensei reached retirement from the post office; his children had grown safely — the gate opened to new members (Ide’s acceptance likely influenced this too).

(I have written elsewhere.) Some time after I joined, drinking, Nakamura Sensei suddenly said: “Tenshinryu is really a school you must not teach to people.”

In his twenties, while his predecessor lived, a disciple used a wooden sword on hoodlums in a park — police matter. Called to **Mitaka Police Station**, he was **forbidden to instruct Tenshinryu**, he believed.

So legally instructing Tenshinryu was illegal — he thought.

It was admonition to youth; no such legal binding force.

(We later confirmed with Mitaka Police.)

Yet for nearly half a century he believed it — teaching only children and workplace friends “unofficially,” embu allowed because not forbidden — hence **no disciples** on the phone (fearing police might call to check).

He stubbornly refused a homepage for that reason. I took about a year after joining to persuade creation — then came this confession.

Told at a neighborhood izakaya: “Police have no such legal power; it has no legal effect” — his face of anticlimax, shock, disbelief, and *what were all those years of worry?* — I still remember.

Later I told him we had called police to confirm; he was truly freed from a long curse.

Later he admitted: **he** had fought, not the disciple — hard to say after I had repeatedly admonished that fighting and duels are unacceptable today.

— Kuwami Masakumo
