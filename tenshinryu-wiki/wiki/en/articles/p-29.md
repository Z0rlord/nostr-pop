# On the Culture of Gō (Art Names)

*2018-10-02* — Traditional culture

## What is *gō*?

**Gō** (号) originated in continental China and took root in Japan. It corresponds to what we might call a nickname, common name, or pen name — but specifically the name used by someone who practices an **art** (*gei*) in that activity.

The origin is often traced to **Ouyang Xiu** (1007–1072) of Northern Song China. Personal nicknames and common names existed far earlier, but as “a title a cultured person applied to himself,” Ouyang Xiu is said to have first taken *Zuiweng* (Drunken Old Man), later *Liuyi Jushi* (Retiree of the Six Ones). Many famous literati followed; the fashion spread and settled.

Japan adopted it across fields: painters — *gagō*; kyōka poets — *kyōmyō*; haiku poets — *haimei* / *haigō*; performers — stage names; reciters — *gingō*. Novelists use pen names; today manga artists and illustrators use pen names routinely.

**Gagō** (雅号) is well known — art names across literature, painting, calligraphy, and more. Rather “an elegant name,” interpret it as “a name used across refined letters and arts broadly.”

Because *gō* spread so widely, some historical figures are known by *gō* rather than **imina** (諱, formal name) or **azana** (字). **Yoshida Shōin**’s “Shōin” is a *gō*; his imina was Norikata — imina were special and not used in daily life.

Such culture in the Sinosphere is called **jitsumei keihizoku** (実名敬避俗) — avoiding the real name in favor of *azana*, common names, and *gō*, complicating Japanese history.

Surnames too often changed; in study of martial transmission documents, different names sometimes prove the same person.

**Katsushika Hokusai** is said to have changed art names some thirty times in his life. A *gō* is not absolutely immutable.

## *Dōgō* and *shitsugō*

Less familiar: **dōgō** (堂号) — elegant or shop names with *dō* (堂). Kyogokudō in Kyogoku Natsuhiko’s fiction is one kind. Often used for shop names. Early Edo monk **Shōkōdō Shōjō**’s hermitage “Shōkōdō” shows long use. With *sai* (斎) it becomes **saigō** (斎号); used for a room, **shitsugō** (室号). Sometimes adopted directly as one’s own *gō*.

## *Bugō*

When martial artists use *gō* today we call it **bugō** (武号). When the term began is unclear; likely not in Edo — they probably said simply “*gō*.” Modern bugei uses **bugō** for clarity; Tenshinryu follows.

## Nakamura Tenshin Shike

**Tenshin** — 9th Shike Nakamura Tenshin’s name — is **not** his birth name, nor an ancient hereditary title passed with the school from its founding.

8th Shike Ishii had **no bugō**, it is said. Not bugō but bushi custom — no need for a special art name.

When Nakamura Shike succeeded Ishii, he received **Tenshin** with the words: “Your real name is precious from your parents; cherish it.” Another reason for adopting **Tenshin** as bugō: to preserve Tenshinryu Hyoho, then little valued, with almost no one studying it.

Today koryū bujutsu holds status as traditional culture; in Ishii’s era, apart from young Nakamura, no serious disciples remained.

Nakamura Shike, born 1943, began with Ishii (born 1892) around age sixteen, ~1959 — not immediately postwar but early high growth; few looked back at samurai arts. Ishii’s son had no interest; bujutsu would not continue in the family.

At the brink of art and skill rotting with the body, young Nakamura entered as literally the **sole inheritor**. “**A person one generation; a name for posterity**” — at least leave the name to later ages. Hence **Tenshin**; disciples call him **Tenshin Sensei**. This blog will use that form.

2nd-generation **Kunitomi Chuzaemon** used **Kyūshin** as *gō*. The Kunitomi house continued the name for generations with Tenshinryu, blurring official generation counts.

By transmission names alone Ishii was 8th; actually Kunitomi Chuzaemon’s full name continued three to four generations — Ishii said he might be 10th or 11th. Nakamura might be 11th or 12th; I might be 12th or 13th — yet we use the count of names in the documents.

## Kuwami Masakumo’s bugō

10th Shike **Kuwami Masakumo** — often called a fierce name — is also **bugō**.

Normally *gō* is used alone, appended to the given name, or replaces the given name; changing **surname** is rare. Change everything and it becomes more **stage name** than bugō.

Yet Tenshin Sensei pondered many days, even the surname, and granted this bugō for activity as a Tenshinryu swordsman; for now we use it including the surname.

Historically, not so strange — discussed below.

## Granting bugō to disciples

After sufficient years, Tenshin Sensei grants **bugō** to disciples — from Ishii’s teaching: real names are precious from parents; use *gō* in martial activity so the real name is not stained.

One may choose one’s own *gō*, but receiving it from the teacher as certification has special value. Too personal a taste risks “documentary names” or flashy “kirakira names” — though being *gō*, perhaps we need not be so nervous.

## Historical names

“Kirakira names” are a modern social issue; Edo was no slouch — **Gakyō rōjin manji**, **Tetsubō nuranura** (Hokusai’s names — the latter for shunga). **Chiyarikurō** — Tokugawa Ieyasu’s grandfather Matsudaira Kiyoyasu gave retainer Nagasaka Nobumasa the nickname **Blood Spear Kurō** — originally **Tea Kurō**, changed for wordplay: “never a spear without blood on the battlefield,” says *Kansei chōshū shokafu*.

Ieyasu has many anecdotes; the Yonekitsu family of hatamoto were named **Umeboshi no Suke** for generations by the divine lord — read **Hoya no Suke**, not “pickled plum aide”: “Your face resembles umeboshi; I give you the art name Umeboshi no Suke” — harsh, yet used for generations. See *Samurai to Yakuza* (Chikuma bunko) by Ujiie Kazuhito for why **Hoya no Suke** and other names Ieyasu gave.

**Oguri Mataichirō** earned **Mataichi** from repeated “first spear” feats. **Ōtsuka Jinzaburō** received **Matōnai** from “again unrivaled work” disarming a spear. Names and even surnames were changed.

Aizu hatamoto **Narabayashi Tomo** famously called himself **Demon Outlaw Left Guard** — reproached by peers.

## Cultural research and preservation

Name culture alone runs deep. Transmitting technique as koryū matters — but valuing the art means understanding the soil and customs behind it; Tenshinryu thinks so.

Not every disciple must study this; each has their own feelings — what matters is daily keiko. Yet willing disciples may pursue cultural research and preservation including such historical facts.

Until barely 150 years ago, topknots and hakama, two-sword samurai walked towns — yet we scarcely know that reality. Some say Meiji government spread Tokugawa rule as a dark age and hid its truth — slightly conspiratorial, but cultural rupture was severe; research remains needed.
