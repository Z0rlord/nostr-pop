# On Shinanjo and Practice Halls

*2018-10-02* — Traditional culture

See also [[dojo/shinanjo-tradition|Shinanjo & practice halls (wiki page)]].

## The name *dōjō*

Today we commonly call a martial training hall a **dōjō**, but that usage spread mainly **after Meiji**, it is said.

Originally *dōjō* is a Buddhist term: a translation of Sanskrit *Bodhimandala*, “the place where the Buddha attained enlightenment.” It came to mean a place of Buddhist training.

Before Meiji, spiritual cultivation in Japanese **bugei** was sought under the aim that a warrior (members of other classes also studied) should control mind and body in the narrow space between life and death and fully express skill. Conduct as a *shi* (or as a person) was taught within the framework of *shidō* formulated through Neo-Confucianism and the like.

Some Edo-period movements sought moral education through martial arts, but the thorough transformation came with Meiji.

Kano Jigorō — founder of the Kodokan and an educator — preserved declining traditional bugei by reorganizing multiple jujutsu schools into **judo**, making it a pillar of modern physical education as Japan rushed toward modernization. That epoch-making shift was not limited to jujutsu → judo.

The **Dai Nippon Butokukai** appeared; kenjutsu (or *gekken*) became **kendō**, kyūjutsu became **kyūdō**, and bujutsu / bugei were unified under the name **budō**. Traditional bugei became the basis for a modern Japanese “Way” combining physical and moral education through training and competition.

In step with this trend, halls formerly called **keikoba**, **enbu-ba**, **gekken-ba**, **shinanjo**, **bukan**, and the like came to be called **dōjō** — a name suited to pursuing and nurturing the Way of character completion.

## The signboard

Ishii Sensei (previous headmaster) treasured Tenshinryu transmission and often said, “Teach as you were taught.” Transmission includes not only technique but the phrases the school uses.

Nakamura Sensei also values transmission and still uses school terminology in its original meaning and wording.

Including the shift of the Buddhist word *dōjō* to mean a budō training hall, Ishii Sensei transmitted this history; Tenshinryu **basically does not use the name *dōjō*** even now.

This photo is a Tenshinryu Hyoho signboard. We regrettably have no dedicated shinanjo at present, so it stands at the headmaster’s home. It was made to Ishii Sensei’s design.

The intent of using the word *juku* (塾) is unclear, but in choosing **shinanjo** rather than *dōjō* or *keikoba*, Ishii Sensei’s feeling and Tenshinryu’s character appear — and Nakamura Sensei inherited that unchanged.

We may call a venue *keikoba* in daily speech, but strictly it is not merely a place to drill: it is a **shinanjo**, a place to **transmit** the martial arts. **Keiko** (稽古) is what each person does individually; the hall is for *shinan* (指南, guidance).

## *Mitadori keiko* (見盗り稽古)

Many schools say “steal and learn” (*nusunde obiero*). Tenshinryu uses the term **mitadori keiko** (見盗り稽古) to stress the spirit of self-study and self-acquisition.

Generally **mitadori keiko** (見取り稽古) is written with “observe” and widely means learning by watching — observation and imitation.

Tenshinryu always uses “steal” (盗) to demand **serious** observation. Left unchecked, watching becomes **mitore keiko** (見蕩れ稽古) — dazed sightseeing, not training. Even when viewing another school’s embu, attend with the mind of a practitioner, not an audience.

Recently “stealing technique” is sometimes linked to theft and avoided as a vulgar phrase. In Tenshinryu it is a **self-warning** and an important word about inner preparation.

If your stance is “teach me…” — passive reception — the art never becomes yours. They say “wrestle sumo in someone else’s loincloth”; a technique merely taught remains another’s loincloth. Each person must refine their own art through personal effort.

Bujutsu of the warrior class was studied for duty, a man’s path staking life; whether to train deeply was one’s inner affair, not a matter of debating teaching methods.

Improving teaching methods is the teacher’s duty; the disciple follows silently.

## The teaching of *san mōka*

“**A fool who still cannot do it after being taught three times**” was Ishii Sensei’s saying, it is said.

The “three times” is not three attempts in one lesson.

First instruction: learn the sequence of the technique. Until the next lesson, drill it thoroughly. Second instruction: corrections — you should already move comfortably, but mistakes and poor movement are pointed out. Third instruction: near completion — you should be able to train independently without further teaching.

That is completion of **instruction**, not completion of the **technique**. Thereafter you acquire depth yourself and pursue free application and mastery.

This is an ideal and a mindset. Even in Edo times, demanding it fully was difficult. If you truly expelled everyone who failed after three lessons, you would have no disciples. It is teaching to **hold that posture**.

Disciples without that preparation are called **san mōka** (三莫迦); these teachings are **the teaching of san mōka**.

## Practice today

Tenshinryu still values this mindset and encourages independent practice “as each person can,” but we cannot expect Edo-warrior-level effort while holding modern jobs — the age of the warrior ended; people entered the gate from their own motives.

Guidance alone, with keiko left entirely to individuals, would not transmit technique; disciples would not improve and might quit.

For several years now, when we borrow sports centers or budō halls, we treat them as **keikoba** and set time for guided practice together. We adapt to the present while **not** discarding inherited phrases like “steal and learn” because the **wording sounds bad**.

We may not embody the Edo warrior literally, but we do not spare effort to transmit that spirit. Warriors differed individually; hence admonitions passed down as oral teaching.

In former guidance halls, questions were not encouraged; there were teachings such as “do not show your teeth in keiko.” Today we welcome questions in practice — keiko should be serious, but also **enjoyable**.
