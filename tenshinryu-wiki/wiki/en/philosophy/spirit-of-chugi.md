# On the Spirit of Chūgi (忠義)
**The spirit of loyalty and righteous service** — a lesson for Tenshinryu ONLINE members by [[people/kuwami-masakumo|Kuwami Masakumo]] (10th Shike), originally posted in the [[community/tenshinryu-hyoho-facebook|Tenshinryu Hyoho Official Community]] (Facebook, 2026-06-26).

> **Audience:** Tenshinryu ONLINE members. This essay connects Confucian **chūgi** (忠義), selected **Hagakure** (葉隠) passages, and the philosophical depth behind Tenshinryu **bugei** (武藝).

---

## Kuwami Shike's introduction

One way of thinking from former times that is profoundly lacking in modern Japan is the spirit of **chūgi** (忠義). Of course, there are not only one, but many such lost ways of thinking. In this lesson, however, I will explain one of them: the spirit of chūgi (忠義).

### What is chūgi?

**Chūgi** (忠義) means to devote one's heart and strength to the lord, master, or country one serves. This is **chū** (忠). At the same time, it must accord with humanity, morality, and righteousness. This is **gi** (義). In traditional Confucian thought, chūgi was regarded as one of the central virtues.

When discussing the relationship between Confucian thought and Japan, attention is often placed on the **Edo period** (江戸時代, 1603–1868). Tokugawa Ieyasu (徳川家康) employed Hayashi Razan (林羅山) and adopted **Shushigaku** (朱子学), or Neo-Confucianism.

However, Confucianism had already entered Japan during the **Asuka period** (飛鳥時代, 592–710) and **Nara period** (奈良時代, 710–794). From that time onward, it continued to influence Japanese spirituality and ethics in various ways.

In the Edo period especially, in order to maintain order during a long age of peace, chūgi and loyalty were sometimes placed above **kō** (孝), filial piety toward one's family. However, this does not mean that loyalty was neglected before the Edo period.

The **Sengoku period** (戦国時代, c. 1467–c. 1615) is often imagined as an age of **gekokujō** (下剋上), when lower-ranking people overthrew their superiors, and betrayal could occur at any time. Yet if lord and vassal truly lived in such constant suspicion, it would have been impossible even to go onto the battlefield together.

In reality, many documents from the Sengoku period also emphasize the importance of the lord–vassal relationship. Therefore, we must understand that chūgi between lord and retainer did not begin in the Edo period.

The activities of the **Shirin-dan** (士林団) began before it formally came under the authority of the **ōmetsuke** (大目付). In 1632, **Yagyū Munenori** (柳生宗矩) was appointed **sōmetsuke** (総目付), but the origins go further back, to the small **Shirin-gumi** (士林組) that began after Yagyū Munenori entered the service of the Divine Lord, Tokugawa Ieyasu (徳川家康), in 1594.

The philosophical background of **Tenshinryu** (天心流) existed before the Edo period. However, unless one understands these Confucian ideas, it becomes difficult to understand the samurai's awareness of duty and responsibility. As a result, one merely traces the surface of the techniques and cannot easily reach their core.

> The techniques exist because there was a mission and purpose behind the samurai.

In order for the techniques of Tenshinryu to possess true depth and weight, one must understand this spirit of chūgi: the resolve to devote oneself in loyalty.

In this article, in order to understand the samurai spirituality of chūgi, I would like to introduce selected episodes concerning loyalty from the famous work **Hagakure** (葉隠).

## About Hagakure (葉隠)

**Hagakure** (葉隠) is a work compiled from the teachings of **Yamamoto Tsunetomo** (山本常朝), a samurai of the **Saga domain** (佐賀藩). These teachings were recorded by **Tashiro Tsuramoto** (田代陣基).

Yamamoto Tsunetomo lived from the early to middle Edo period. After the death of his lord, **Nabeshima Mitsushige** (鍋島光茂), he became a monk and later spoke about his views on the samurai way and the attitude of service.

The most famous phrase is:

> **The Way of the Samurai is found in death.**

Many people may know this phrase. Because this record instructed the reader to burn it after memorizing it, and because it was treated as a forbidden text within the Nabeshima domain, it has often been misunderstood. Some have falsely believed that it preached an abnormal theory of the samurai, different from Edo-period **bushidō**.

However, most people have not actually known the contents of Hagakure. That is probably why such misunderstandings arose. In reality, Hagakure merely conveys the general bushidō of the Edo period.

At the same time, it contains many internal matters concerning the castle and the domain lord. Speaking or writing about such internal matters was generally taboo. Therefore, the true reason it could not be made public was that the work contained many such sensitive details.

The original manuscript has been lost, but several copies remain. It was published in the late **Meiji period** (明治時代, 1868–1912) and became known to the public. Later, parts of it were used to strengthen militaristic ideology, and after the war it was temporarily treated as a banned or dangerous book. These later developments are one cause of the misunderstandings surrounding Hagakure.

In truth, Hagakure is not only a book of samurai teachings. It is also a valuable work that allows us to glimpse the daily life of samurai in former times. In that sense, it can be called essential reading for understanding the Edo period.

However, it is a vast work, and its people, places, and contents are extremely difficult. Therefore, in this article, I will focus on chūgi and loyalty, and introduce selected passages from **Kikigaki Daiichi** (聞書第一) in an accessible translation.

**Full text (kakikudashi genbun):** [Saga Library Database — Hagakure Book 1](https://www.sagalibdb.jp/hagakure/list02)

## Hagakure (葉隠) — Kikigaki Daiichi (聞書第一): passages and commentary

### Hagakure (葉隠), Kikigaki Daiichi (聞書第一), Section 16 — Reflecting on one's own fault

**Passage from Hagakure (葉隠)**

> This is the content of advice that was given orally to a certain person. It concerns the matter of resenting one's lord and superiors while being a **rōnin** (浪人).
>
> There was once a rōnin who realized that the fault had actually been his own, and five or six years later he returned to service. When he had been ordered to do something the first time, he refused. But the second time, he accepted the order and submitted a written oath.
>
> The point is this: if he had broken his pride when he first refused, or when he shaved his head and withdrew, that would have been admirable.
>
> In the same way, regarding a rōnin, as long as he does not know his own fault, returning to service is impossible.
>
> Even now, if he only thinks, "There is no mercy," or "I hate that person," and burns with resentment in his heart, he will incur the hatred of **Tendō** (天道), the Way of Heaven.
>
> Regarding the reputation of a certain person, it was said, "This is divine punishment." It is not that others refuse to release him. The fault lies with him alone. He must reflect on this. Otherwise, returning to service will be completely impossible.

**Kuwami Shike's commentary**

The teaching is that, whatever happens, one should look for fault within oneself and reflect.

If one cannot do even that, then even if one is asked to return to service—that is, even if one is invited back or recalled—one has no qualification to return.

If one is made a rōnin or dismissed, it is natural to resent it. However, as will be shown later, Hagakure teaches that one must not think, "I was made a rōnin." One must instead think, "I was allowed to become a rōnin." Unless one can think in this way, one cannot fulfill one's service.

At all times, the lord comes first. Right and wrong are always understood from the position that the lord is right and oneself is wrong.

The inability to admit one's own fault is the same in every age.

Also, it is rare that one side is 100 percent right and the other side is 100 percent wrong. Even so, Hagakure demands that one take 100 percent of the blame upon oneself. Otherwise, even if one is forgiven, one has no qualification to return to service.

### Hagakure (葉隠), Kikigaki Daiichi (聞書第一), Section 32 — Lord before gods and Buddhas

**Passage from Hagakure (葉隠)**

> Every morning, one should first bow to one's lord and parents. After that, one should bow to the **ujigami** (氏神), the clan or local deity, and to the various Buddhas.
>
> If one serves one's lord with care, one's parents will also be pleased, and the Buddhas and gods will surely accept it.
>
> As for myself, I know nothing except thinking of my lord. Since I place my lord alone first, I constantly attend to his person and serve without leaving him even for a moment.
>
> Also, a woman should first regard her husband as she would regard her lord.

**Kuwami Shike's commentary**

It is truly interesting that this teaching places the lord, meaning chūgi, and the parents, meaning kō, before worship of the gods and Buddhas.

Japan has been described as a divine country. The **Nihon Shoki** (日本書紀) says, "There is a divine country in the east. It is called Japan." The **Jinnō Shōtōki** (神皇正統記, 1339) says, "Great Japan is the land of the gods."

In such a country, to say that one should revere the lord even before the gods is an extremely strong statement.

If one places the lord above all else, that also leads to filial piety. In turn, the gods and Buddhas rejoice in this.

### Hagakure (葉隠), Kikigaki Daiichi (聞書第一), Section 36 — The true retainer

**Passage from Hagakure (葉隠)**

> When one looks at retainers of the present day, their view of things is extremely low. Like the eyes of a thief, they look only at their own profit and loss.
>
> Most are concerned with their own benefit, advancement, showing off their cleverness, or appearing somewhat calm. They are merely protecting themselves.
>
> However, the true retainer offers his life and body to his lord. He becomes like a ghost who has already died while still alive. That is, while still living, he has already cast himself away and fully offered his life and body to his lord.
>
> At all times, day and night, he thinks of his lord. He arranges matters and reports them. He fixes his eyes on strengthening the country and the house.
>
> In this, there is no distinction between high and low rank. Those above and those below must firmly settle themselves in this resolve. Even if the gods and Buddhas themselves urged otherwise, one must have such resolve that one would not waver in the slightest.

**Kuwami Shike's commentary**

This is, in a way, like the expression of a maiden in love.

Day and night, one thinks of the lord and offers both body and heart.

Even if the gods and Buddhas appeared and spoke words contrary to chūgi, one would cast them aside without the slightest hesitation. That is the level of resolve being described.

The gods and Buddhas are important, but they do not surpass the lord.

If this is true chūgi, then how many samurai could truly be called samurai? Needless to say, only a very small number of warriors reached such a state.

However, unless one intends to establish one's loyalty at an almost absurd level, even half of it will not arise.

### Hagakure (葉隠), Kikigaki Daiichi (聞書第一), Section 88 — Reward and true service

**Passage from Hagakure (葉隠)**

> There was once a man who had served seriously for many years, and he thought that a suitable reward would surely be given to him. Then a letter concerning official business arrived, and those around him congratulated him in advance.
>
> However, what he was actually ordered to receive was not the great reward he had expected, but an increase in official rice allowance. Everyone was surprised. Even so, because it was a formal command, they expressed their congratulations.
>
> The man then looked deeply dissatisfied and said: "This is a shameful outcome. In the end, I must be someone who is of no use in service, and that is why I have been treated in this way. I would like to refuse this and withdraw."
>
> His close acquaintances tried in various ways to calm him, and in the end he continued to serve.
>
> However, this was entirely because he lacked the resolve for **hōkō** (奉公), service. He was merely using his own honor and pride as the standard.
>
> Leaving aside whether the reward is large or small, even if one were reduced from **samurai** (侍) to **ashigaru** (足軽), or even ordered to commit **seppuku** (切腹) despite having committed no crime, the one who accepts this with even greater spirit is the true hereditary retainer.
>
> To say, "I have lost face," is entirely selfishness.
>
> One must receive this point very deeply and settle one's heart. However, in the case of an especially difficult or extraordinary person, another form of treatment may be necessary.

**Kuwami Shike's commentary**

He expected something on his own, became disappointed on his own, and finally tried to resign from his post on his own.

He made it appear as if he were ashamed of his own uselessness, but in reality he was merely accusing his lord of giving him an unfair evaluation. Such a thing is not true chūgi.

If one is loyal when one receives recognition, but withdraws when one is not evaluated as expected, that is nothing more than an ordinary relationship of self-interest.

Certainly, in the **Kamakura period** (鎌倉時代, 1185–1333), the warrior class was described through **go-on to hōkō** (御恩と奉公), "reward and service." In other words, it was a kind of contractual relationship.

However, from there, the lord–vassal relationship of the warrior class evolved, so to speak.

Of course, samurai originally placed great importance on honor and pride. From that perspective, the words and actions of this samurai are understandable.

Nevertheless, Hagakure teaches that one is truly a retainer only when one places the lord and chūgi above even samurai honor and pride.

### Hagakure (葉隠), Kikigaki Daiichi (聞書第一), Section 93 — Resolving for rōnin or seppuku

**Passage from Hagakure (葉隠)**

> Someone once said: "Everyone thinks that becoming a rōnin is the greatest possible disaster. When the time approaches, people become terribly discouraged and exhausted. However, when one actually becomes a rōnin, it is not so terrible. It is not as bad as one imagined beforehand. Now, I feel that I could become a rōnin again."
>
> This is reasonable.
>
> Death is the same. If one is ordinarily resolved to die and accustomed to death, when the time comes, one can die with peace of mind.
>
> Disaster is not as great as one imagines beforehand.
>
> It is foolish to suffer by thinking anxiously about a future that has not yet arrived.
>
> A retainer should resolve in advance that the final destination of service is either becoming a rōnin or committing seppuku.

**Kuwami Shike's commentary**

This means that one should assume the worst in advance.

One may wonder whether it is possible to devote oneself to chūgi if one has already accepted that the end of service may be rōnin status or seppuku.

However, this is not resignation. It is the settling of one's resolve.

To establish chūgi, such resolve and courage are necessary.

### Hagakure (葉隠), Kikigaki Daiichi (聞書第一), Section 108 — Bearing and fūtei (風体)

**Passage from Hagakure (葉隠)**

> As for training one's **fūtei** (風体)—one's appearance, posture, bearing, and facial expression—it is best to correct it daily by looking into a mirror.
>
> When I was thirteen, I was to arrange my hairstyle, and I stayed away from public view for about a year. Before that, members of my family had said, "That person has a clever-looking face, so he must be careful, or he will fail. The lord especially dislikes those who look clever."
>
> Therefore, I decided to use this opportunity to correct my facial expression, and I constantly looked into a mirror to adjust it.
>
> After a year had passed and I appeared before people, everyone said that I had **korō-shitaji** (虚労下地), meaning a weak, sickly, and exhausted-looking face.
>
> However, I thought that this was precisely the foundation of service.
>
> A person whose cleverness shows on his face will not be accepted by others.
>
> Unless one has calmness and weight, one's bearing is not good.
>
> It is best to be reverent, subdued, mature, and quiet in tone.

**Kuwami Shike's commentary**

This is one of the relatively famous episodes in Hagakure.

What matters is not whether he actually changed his face. What matters is that he "came to the thought" of changing his facial expression for the sake of service.

He did not say, "This is the face I was born with, so it cannot be helped." Moreover, even though no actual problem had yet occurred in his service, he still resolved to change his face for the sake of service.

Such a spirit can be called abnormal by ordinary standards.

Precisely because one goes beyond ordinary standards, it becomes hōkō.

### Hagakure (葉隠), Kikigaki Daiichi (聞書第一), Section 111 — True admonition (kangen 諫言)

**Passage from Hagakure (葉隠)**

> There are various ways to offer **kangen** (諫言), admonition, to one's lord.
>
> True admonition, born from sincere aspiration, is done so that those around do not know of it. The proper way is to correct the lord's error while avoiding injury to his feelings.
>
> The loyalty of **Hosokawa Yoriyuki** (細川頼之) is an example of this.
>
> Long ago, when a certain lord attempted to make an unscheduled detour during a journey, a certain elder retainer heard of it and said, "I speak while casting away my life. The journey is already delayed, and for you to make a further detour is truly improper."
>
> That man then said to those around him, "I now take my leave." He purified himself with water, put on a white **katabira** (帷子), and appeared before his lord in a form prepared for death.
>
> Before long, when he came out, he again said to those around him, "My lord has understood what I said, and this is my deepest satisfaction. That I have been able to see you all once more is a mysterious happiness."
>
> However, all of this was conduct that exposed the lord's fault to the world, made his own loyalty stand out, and displayed his own authority.
>
> This sort of thing is probably common among men from other provinces.

**Kuwami Shike's commentary**

At first glance, this episode may make one wonder what the problem is.

To point something out to one's lord while prepared even for seppuku seems like sincere chūgi and hōkō. It feels like something modern people could not do.

However, Hagakure condemns this as clever admonition, as performance-like admonition meant to appeal to those around him. It is not born from true chūgi. It is an act that looks down upon the lord.

If one admonishes the lord after making it known to those around, then if the lord changes his mind, the effect of that admonition is displayed publicly. It becomes an appeal showing the retainer's own loyalty.

Also, although he appears to show resolve, he has not truly entrusted his own body to the lord.

Why? Because he has prepared for seppuku without having been ordered to do so. This is nothing other than selfish conduct.

One should simply offer admonition without others knowing. If the lord accepts it, that is fortunate. If the lord gives some punishment, one should accept it calmly. If the lord orders seppuku, then one should make preparations after receiving that command. That is the proper order.

### Hagakure (葉隠), Kikigaki Daiichi (聞書第一), Section 114 — Shinigurui (死狂い) and bushidō (武士道)

**Passage from Hagakure (葉隠)**

> Lord Naoshige (直茂公) said: "Bushidō (武士道) is the resolve to become mad with death. Even striking down a single person may be impossible for several dozen men."
>
> Great deeds cannot be accomplished with an ordinary level of seriousness.
>
> One must become mad and reach the state of **shinigurui** (死狂い), death-frenzy.
>
> Also, in bushidō, if judgment or calculation of gain and loss comes first, one has already fallen behind.
>
> There is no need to explain **chū** (忠), loyalty, or **kō** (孝), filial piety, through reasoning.
>
> In the martial way, the first thing is simply the resolve to become mad with death. Within that, loyalty and filial piety are naturally included.

**Kuwami Shike's commentary**

What matters here is the original phrase: "Great deeds cannot be accomplished by seriousness. One must become mad and enter death-frenzy."

Mere seriousness is not much. Madness is necessary first.

**Shinigurui** (死狂い) is often translated as "desperate struggle." It means acting without fear of death, with complete single-mindedness.

It is precisely within such a state that chū (忠) naturally arises.

Something beyond reason is indispensable to true chūgi. Judgment can sometimes become an obstacle.

When chūgi does not arise from the depths, and poor cleverness enters instead, performance-like loyalty such as that described in the previous section becomes widespread.

This is a difficult matter.

### Hagakure (葉隠), Kikigaki Daiichi (聞書第一), Section 125 — Raising good retainers

**Passage from Hagakure (葉隠)**

> Within the house, it is important to train, guide, and shape people so that good retainers and good servants may be raised.
>
> Those who have aspiration should be properly taught and guided.
>
> If one can serve the lord not only through one's own work, but also through the people one has raised, that is truly one's deepest satisfaction.

**Kuwami Shike's commentary**

Raising good retainers and good servants within one's house is also one form of chūgi.

If one thinks in this way, the hardship of raising people should be nothing.

To think that it is enough for oneself alone to be a loyal person becomes disloyalty.

Unless one constantly thinks about what truly serves the lord, one's loyalty will remain merely self-satisfying.

### Hagakure (葉隠), Kikigaki Daiichi (聞書第一), Section 126 — Lord and retainer vs. family discord

**Passage from Hagakure (葉隠)**

> When relations become poor between the retired former head and the present head, or between father and son, or between brothers, the cause is desire.
>
> The proof is that discord does not occur between lord and retainer.

**Kuwami Shike's commentary**

One simply devotes oneself earnestly to the lord. There is no ego there. There is only the spirit of **messhi hōkō** (滅私奉公), selfless service.

Therefore, there is no discord in the lord–vassal relationship, while discord arises in family relationships because they are filled with selfishness.

This is an extraordinary theory.

One naturally wants to object: "But surely personalities can be incompatible," or "Discord between lord and retainer surely occurred constantly in the actual Edo period."

However, regardless of what actual lord–vassal relationships were like, Hagakure teaches paradoxically that the relationship *ought* to be this way.

If one then says, "But even between lord and retainer…," then according to this teaching, a samurai who has discord with his lord is a rogue lacking loyalty.

### Hagakure (葉隠), Kikigaki Daiichi (聞書第一), Section 128 — Fall seven times, rise eight

**Passage from Hagakure (葉隠)**

> To lose composure merely because one has become a rōnin is outrageous.
>
> In the time of Lord Katsushige (勝茂公), people often said: "Unless one has experienced becoming a rōnin seven times, one cannot be called a true retainer. Fall seven times, rise eight."
>
> **Narutomi Hyōgo** (成富兵庫) is also said to have become a rōnin seven times.
>
> This should be understood like the mechanism of an **okiagari ningyō** (起き上がり人形), a doll that rises again after being knocked down.
>
> The lord, too, may deliberately command such circumstances in order to test his retainers.

**Kuwami Shike's commentary**

One wants to say, "Do not ask the impossible."

Becoming a rōnin essentially means being dismissed. One becomes rootless, loses one's **roku** (禄)—stipend, salary, and means of livelihood—loses the official residence granted by the domain, and one's family is cast onto the road.

Being dismissed in order to test one's loyalty is unbearable.

Normally, "fall seven times, rise eight" would mean that one should work hard in another path. One might begin a samurai business, or return to farming through **kinō** (帰農). Originally this means returning to one's former status as a farmer, but here it refers to a samurai abandoning his status or becoming a rōnin and becoming a farmer.

One would normally think it means seeking another path.

However, as expected of Nabeshima samurai, Hagakure does not show such a gentle or soft path.

If one has true loyalty and chūgi, then even if one is given **itoma** (暇), dismissal or leave from service, one must simply endure until one is called back. One must always keep the **sanshaku no shūsui** (三尺の秋水) from rusting.

This phrase is another way of referring to the sword. **Sanshaku** (三尺) means about 90 centimeters, and **shūsui** (秋水) means a blade polished cold and clear like autumn water.

This is the true samurai.

One may think, "I would never want a lord who dismisses retainers in order to test them." But Hagakure teaches that one must endure even by thinking, "This may be a trial."

### Hagakure (葉隠), Kikigaki Daiichi (聞書第一), Section 129 — Seven rebirths

**Passage from Hagakure (葉隠)**

> There is a saying that the **hirakuchi** (ひらくち), a local name for the **mamushi** (蝮) pit viper, returns to its original form even if it is burned seven times.
>
> In the same way, I too have a great wish.
>
> Even if I am reborn seven times, I will be born as a samurai of my lord's house and surely accomplish my deepest desire. One should strongly impress this upon oneself.

**Kuwami Shike's commentary**

One should strongly, deeply wish to serve the same lord, or the same lord's house, no matter how many times one is reborn.

Modern people may think, "Even if you wish for that, nothing can be done about it."

However, only when one has a desire so strong that one yearns for it in this way can true chūgi be cultivated.

There is a saying: **shujū wa sanze** (主従は三世), "lord and retainer are bound through three lives."

It is also said: "Husband and wife are one life; lord and retainer are three lives."

The bond between husband and wife belongs to this present life. But between lord and retainer, there is a karmic connection across past, present, and future.

This idea is also used in the teacher–student relationship: **shitei wa sanze** (師弟は三世), or **shitei wa sanze no chigiri** (師弟は三世の契り), "teacher and student are bound through three lives."

Of course, Hagakure knows this and still says, "No, seven lives."

If we trace this back, it may also call to mind **shichishō mezoku** (七生滅賊), a phrase said to have been sworn by **Kusunoki Masasue** (楠木正季), the younger brother of **Kusunoki Masashige** (楠木正成), at the Battle of Minatogawa (湊川の戦い, 1336) during the **Nanboku-chō period** (南北朝時代, 1336–1392).

According to the **Taiheiki** (太平記), which was formed during the **Muromachi period** (室町時代, 1336–1573), he is said to have declared, "Even if we are reborn seven times, we shall destroy the enemies of the Imperial Court," and then taken his own life.

Following this, the phrase **shichishō hōkoku** (七生報国), "to repay the country through seven lives," was used among soldiers during the period of the Japanese Empire.

The term **shichishō** (七生) itself is a Buddhist term. It teaches that a holy person may be reborn at most seven times in the human or heavenly realms before finally attaining liberation.

From this, in Japan, shichishō came to be used with the sense of "for all eternity" or "throughout the endless future."

### Hagakure (葉隠), Kikigaki Daiichi (聞書第一), Section 130 — Allies for the lord's sake

**Passage from Hagakure (葉隠)**

> According to the thought of Lord Naoshige, a samurai with aspiration should normally associate closely with his colleagues and companions.
>
> For this reason, from samurai down to ashigaru, it is necessary to connect deeply with many people.
>
> If one does so, then in a time of emergency, when one says, "For the sake of our lord, will you not work together with me?" one will know in advance that not merely two or three people, but many people, will agree.
>
> This is the first step toward having good retainers and good allies. As a result, it serves the lord.

**Kuwami Shike's commentary**

This says that becoming close to others within the same house or domain must never be for oneself.

Even that is necessary in order to fulfill one's duty as a retainer or servant. Everything is for the lord.

If one thinks in this way, then whether one personally gets along with another person or not, and whatever problems that person may have, one can endure and connect with him sincerely.

Even becoming close to people is hōkō and duty.

There is a thorough awareness here of being a public person.

Of course, actual samurai did not all become close to one another. There were factions, personal likes and dislikes, compatibility and incompatibility, and grudges.

However, Hagakure teaches that the proper form of the samurai is not like that.

To be unable to become close to others because of personal compatibility or self-interest is, in other words, selfishness. It is not the heart of a retainer.

---

### Hagakure (葉隠), Kikigaki Daiichi (聞書第一), Section 137 — Admonition with one's life (Sagara Kyūma)

**Passage from Hagakure (葉隠)**

> There are those who say, "If I offer my opinion to my lord, it will offend him and cause harm, so I will not speak." But all of this is merely an excuse. If one speaks with the resolve to throw away one's life, the lord will surely understand.
>
> Because one offers an opinion with half-hearted resolve, it offends the lord, and one is rejected before the matter is fully heard.
>
> In the past, **Sagara Kyūma** (相良求馬) strongly admonished his lord against the lord's will. Because of this, he incurred the lord's anger and was ordered to commit seppuku.
>
> Then **Shōno Oribe** (生野織部) and **Yamazaki Kurando** (山崎蔵人) came and asked what had happened. Kyūma said: "To commit seppuku is my deepest satisfaction. However, there is still one thing I have left unsaid. If I die without conveying it, I will regret it even after death. Because of our long friendship, please convey this matter to our lord."
>
> His words reached the lord's ears through the two men. The lord was still angry, but he ordered, "Delay Kyūma's seppuku for a while." In the end, he accepted Kyūma's opinion and pardoned him.

**Kuwami Shike's commentary**

In the modern age, to speak up and give one's opinion is considered a good act. Even if the opinion is shallow, hasty, or off the mark, people often think it should be accepted and praised.

However, in the lord–vassal relationship of former times, even if the admonition was completely proper, one might still be ordered to commit seppuku, as in the example above.

This seems terribly unreasonable. Yet even in modern companies, there are not a few cases where an employee who proposes improvements or points out problems is instead punished or scolded.

Hagakure teaches that one should gladly accept such unreasonable treatment.

The lord has responded to one's words and given a judgment. Whatever that judgment may be, it is something bestowed by the lord one serves. To accept it gladly is true hōkō and true chūgi.

This is an extremely difficult way of thinking to understand.

It may seem like a fanatical idea. In a sense, Hagakure teaches one to serve with a kind of blind faith.

Of course, samurai like Sagara Kyūma were few. Precisely because such warriors were rare, Yamamoto Tsunetomo transmitted this story. It does not mean that all samurai were like this. It is an ideal.

## Kuwami Shike's conclusion

There is no end to the examples, so I will stop here for now.

In this article, I have taken up only part of **Kikigaki Daiichi** (聞書第一). Section 137 is the final section discussed here, but the original text continues to Section 203. And **Hagakure** (葉隠) itself continues for ten books.

It is truly interesting, and I strongly recommend reading it.

It is very difficult, but the full text of the Japanese version, in **kakikudashi genbun** (書き下し原文), can be read online for free at the link below:

**[Saga Library Database — Hagakure](https://www.sagalibdb.jp/hagakure/list02)**

Ideally, one would read the original manuscript in **kuzushiji** (くずし字), cursive classical script. However, even I cannot read it. Old handwritten characters are extremely difficult to decipher.

Now, I believe that Hagakure (葉隠) has been translated and introduced in many languages around the world.

Many of these editions are probably **shōhon** (抄本), abridged editions. A shōhon is not the full original text, but a book that extracts only the important or necessary parts.

Even so, I recommend reading it, even from an abridged edition.

After reading this far, many people may have been reminded of romantic love or **shinjin** (信心), religious faith.

Indeed, deep attachment and love lie at the root of chūgi (忠義).

As I have repeatedly said, this is an ideal. Not all samurai reached such a level. Many samurai surely ended their service with imperfect loyalty.

However, precisely because this almost mad spirit of chūgi was upheld as an ideal, warriors were able to govern. For the sake of their lord, for the sake of the house or domain, for the sake of their own family, and for the sake of the people of the domain, they became examples to the common people. Even amid difficult living conditions, they pursued the proper form of the samurai. In this way, the **bakuhan taisei** (幕藩体制), the shogunate–domain system of the Edo period (江戸時代, 1603–1868), was maintained.

In a life tossed about by ego and selfishness, the ultimate spirit of service—thinking only of the lord and devoting oneself to chūgi for the lord's sake—shows a path without hesitation.

It is not self-interest, nor simply altruism, but perhaps service for the lord.

It is an extremely simple way of thinking. Whether possible or impossible, to take it as a guide for how to live—its lack of hesitation, firm will, and unwavering conviction—can inspire a certain admiration.

Even though science has advanced so greatly, and even though modern people supposedly enjoy mature culture, advanced education, and abundant lives, many people are still lost, almost like spiritual refugees.

It would be somewhat problematic to praise the samurai way and social system of former times without reservation. However, as the saying **onko chishin** (温故知新) teaches—"to study the old and know the new"—learning the words and thoughts of people from the past is deeply meaningful when we consider how we should live and how we may live better. See [[philosophy/onko-chishin|Onko chishin]].

For a samurai serving with single-minded devotion, strength is essential.

What produces that strength is **bugei** (武藝), the martial arts.

When one thinks of chūgi, one establishes a great cause that separates oneself from selfishness. Naturally, one's training ceases to be shallow or temporary.

Training built upon such a sense of mission and resolve cannot end at an easy level.

> Understanding this historical and philosophical background is essential for deeply understanding Tenshinryu (天心流).
