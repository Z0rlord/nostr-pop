# On the Spirit of Chūgi (忠義)

**THE SPIRIT OF LOYALTY AND RIGHTEOUS SERVICE**

## KUWAMI SHIKE'S INTRODUCTION

One way of thinking from former times that is profoundly lacking in modern Japan is the spirit of chūgi (忠義).
Of course, there are not only one, but many such lost ways of thinking. In this lesson, however, I will explain one of them: the spirit of chūgi (忠義).
First, what is chūgi (忠義)?
Chūgi (忠義) means to devote one's heart and strength to the lord, master, or country one serves. This is chū (忠). At the same time, it must accord with humanity, morality, and righteousness. This is gi (義). In traditional Confucian thought, chūgi was regarded as one of the central virtues.
When discussing the relationship between Confucian thought and Japan, attention is often placed on the Edo period (江戸時代, 1603–1868). Tokugawa Ieyasu (徳川家康) employed Hayashi Razan (林羅山) and adopted Shushigaku (朱子学), or Neo-Confucianism.
However, Confucianism had already entered Japan during the Asuka period (飛鳥時代, 592–710) and Nara period (奈良時代, 710–794). From that time onward, it continued to influence Japanese spirituality and ethics in various ways.
In the Edo period especially, in order to maintain order during a long age of peace, chūgi and loyalty were sometimes placed above kō (孝), filial piety toward one's family.
However, this does not mean that loyalty was neglected before the Edo period.
The Sengoku period (戦国時代, c. 1467–c. 1615) is often imagined as an age of gekokujō (下剋上), when lower-ranking people overthrew their superiors, and betrayal could occur at any time. Yet if lord and vassal truly lived in such constant suspicion, it would have been impossible even to go onto the battlefield together.
In reality, many documents from the Sengoku period also emphasize the importance of the lord-vassal relationship. Therefore, we must understand that chūgi between lord and retainer did not begin in the Edo period.
The activities of the Shirin-dan (士林団) began before it formally came under the authority of the ōmetsuke (大目付). In 1632, Yagyū Munenori (柳生宗矩) was appointed sōmetsuke (総目付), but the origins go further back, to the small Shirin-gumi (士林組) that began after Yagyū Munenori entered the service of the Divine Lord, Tokugawa Ieyasu (徳川家康), in 1594.
The philosophical background of Tenshinryu (天心流) existed before the Edo period. However, unless one understands these Confucian ideas, it becomes difficult to understand the samurai's awareness of duty and responsibility. As a result, one merely traces the surface of the techniques and cannot easily reach their core.
The techniques exist because there was a mission and purpose behind the samurai.
In order for the techniques of Tenshinryu to possess true depth and weight, one must understand this spirit of chūgi: the resolve to devote oneself in loyalty.
In this article, in order to understand the samurai spirituality of chūgi, I would like to introduce selected episodes concerning loyalty from the famous work Hagakure (葉隠).

## About Hagakure (葉隠)

Hagakure (葉隠) is a work compiled from the teachings of Yamamoto Tsunetomo (山本常朝), a samurai of the Saga domain (佐賀藩). These teachings were recorded by Tashiro Tsuramoto (田代陣基).
Yamamoto Tsunetomo lived from the early to middle Edo period. After the death of his lord, Nabeshima Mitsushige (鍋島光茂), he became a monk and later spoke about his views on the samurai way and the attitude of service.
The most famous phrase is: "The Way of the Samurai is found in death."
Many people may know this phrase.
Because this record instructed the reader to burn it after memorizing it, and because it was treated as a forbidden text within the Nabeshima domain, it has often been misunderstood. Some have falsely believed that it preached an abnormal theory of the samurai, different from Edo-period bushidō.
However, most people have not actually known the contents of Hagakure. That is probably why such misunderstandings arose. In reality, Hagakure merely conveys the general bushidō of the Edo period.
At the same time, it contains many internal matters concerning the castle and the domain lord. Speaking or writing about such internal matters was generally taboo. Therefore, the true reason it could not be made public was that the work contained many such sensitive details.
The original manuscript has been lost, but several copies remain. It was published in the late Meiji period (明治時代, 1868–1912) and became known to the public. Later, parts of it were used to strengthen militaristic ideology, and after the war it was temporarily treated as a banned or dangerous book. These later developments are one cause of the misunderstandings surrounding Hagakure.
In truth, Hagakure is not only a book of samurai teachings. It is also a valuable work that allows us to glimpse the daily life of samurai in former times. In that sense, it can be called essential reading for understanding the Edo period.
However, it is a vast work, and its people, places, and contents are extremely difficult. Therefore, in this article, I will focus on chūgi and loyalty, and introduce selected passages in an accessible translation.
